# Wikipedia Text Licensing Under GFDL and CC BY-SA 4.0

## Executive summary

Wikipedia text is currently governed, at the platform level, by the Wikimedia Foundation Terms of Use. For text that contributors own, Wikimedia requires dual licensing under CC BY-SA 4.0 and the GFDL with no invariant sections, no front-cover texts, and no back-cover texts; reusers may comply with either license or both. At the same time, today's page footers emphasize CC BY-SA 4.0 and say that "additional terms may apply," which is shorthand for the broader licensing framework reflected in the Terms of Use.[^1]

For most modern reuse of Wikipedia text, CC BY-SA 4.0 is the operationally simpler path. It allows attribution "in any reasonable manner," permits a link to a page containing attribution details, requires an indication of modifications when you share adapted material, and applies share-alike only when you share Adapted Material rather than a mere collection. By contrast, the GFDL was designed for manuals and imposes heavier formalities around license copies, title pages, history sections, and access to a "transparent" copy for modified versions.[^2]

Historically, Wikipedia began under the GFDL, then moved in 2009 to a dual-license structure built around CC BY-SA 3.0 plus GFDL, after the FSF added the time-limited section 11 relicensing mechanism to GFDL 1.3 for qualifying "Massive Multiauthor Collaboration Sites." In 2023, Wikimedia implemented the long-discussed upgrade from CC BY-SA 3.0 to CC BY-SA 4.0 for project text. The migration path therefore runs GFDL → CC BY-SA 3.0 (only through the 2008–2009 relicensing window for qualifying wiki content) and CC BY-SA 3.0 → CC BY-SA 4.0 through CC's later-version mechanism. There is no general reverse path from modern Wikipedia text back into purely GFDL-only workflows, and Wikimedia expressly forbids importing content that is available solely under GFDL.[^3]

The main practical consequence is straightforward: if you reuse Wikipedia text on a website, in a book, or in software documentation, you should usually (a) verify that you are dealing with text rather than separately licensed media, (b) follow the CC BY-SA 4.0 attribution path unless you have a strong reason to use the GFDL, (c) check the page footer, history, and talk page for imported or mixed-license material and any extra attribution notices, and (d) mark modifications clearly if you translate, edit, abridge, or otherwise adapt the text.[^4]

## Governing sources and licensing history

The most important primary sources are the GFDL 1.3 legal text and FAQ from GNU, the CC BY-SA 4.0 legal code and Creative Commons FAQ, and Wikimedia's own Terms of Use, reuse guidance, and relicensing materials. Those are the sources that actually control reuse obligations or explain how Wikimedia expects them to be applied.[^5]

Wikimedia's 2007 Licensing policy established the baseline rule that Wikimedia projects should host only free-content material, while permitting limited non-free local exceptions through project-specific Exemption Doctrine Policies. That resolution matters for reuse because it explains why text and media should generally be expected to be freely reusable unless specifically marked otherwise.[^6]

In December 2007, the Wikimedia Foundation Board formally requested that the GFDL be modified to allow migration by mass-collaborative projects to CC BY-SA, and committed to a later community decision on actual relicensing. The FSF then released GFDL 1.3 in November 2008, and its FAQ explains that the key change was section 11, which allowed certain qualifying wiki material to also be used under CC BY-SA 3.0, provided there were no invariant sections or cover texts and, for externally originated material, it had been incorporated before November 1, 2008. The permission expired after August 1, 2009.[^7]

The 2009 Wikimedia licensing update implemented that pathway. Wikimedia's Q&A makes clear that the intent was dual licensing, not an immediate abandonment of the GFDL: old revisions would be available under both GFDL and CC BY-SA, and new revisions would also be dual licensed, except where external CC BY-SA-only imports prevented GFDL availability. Wikimedia's public announcement states that on June 15, 2009, the English Wikipedia footer and related messages changed from the GFDL as primary content license to CC BY-SA as the primary license for text.[^8]

A second major transition came later. Wikimedia opened a 2016 consultation on upgrading project text from CC BY-SA 3.0 Unported to CC BY-SA 4.0 International, and Wikimedia's legal note explained the main reasons: improved internationalization, clearer attribution rules, explicit database-rights treatment, and a 30-day cure mechanism for violations. Wikimedia then announced in 2023 that the licensing update to CC BY-SA 4.0 had become effective on many projects as part of a Terms of Use update.[^9]

One important caveat is that some on-wiki help pages still reflect older stages of this history. For example, the old "compliant examples" page on English Wikipedia still discusses CC BY-SA 3.0 and shows a last substantive edit from 2010, while Wikimedia's 2023 announcement expressly noted that volunteers would still need to update many relevant policy and interface pages. For reuse analysis, the current Terms of Use, current footer language, and current license texts should therefore be given priority over stale examples.[^10]

## Core legal terms and differences

The GFDL is a copyleft license written for "manual[s], textbook[s], or other functional and useful document[s]," and it grants a world-wide, royalty-free license, unlimited in duration. It expressly permits copying and distributing the document "either commercially or noncommercially." The CC BY-SA 4.0 legal code grants a worldwide, royalty-free, non-sublicensable, non-exclusive, irrevocable license to reproduce and share the licensed material, and Creative Commons' official summary states that it can be used "for any purpose, even commercially."[^15]

The biggest operational difference for reusers is attribution format. Under CC BY-SA 4.0, if you share the material, you must retain creator-identification if supplied, a copyright notice, a license notice, a disclaimer notice, and a URI to the material where reasonably practicable; indicate modifications; and include the license text or a link to it. The license also says those conditions may be satisfied in any reasonable manner depending on medium and context—for example by linking to a page containing the required information. Creative Commons' FAQ further clarifies that, for versions prior to 4.0, the title of the work had to be provided if supplied, but in 4.0 that is no longer a separate required element.[^16]

Under the GFDL, the requirements are much more document-structural. Verbatim copying requires reproducing the license, the copyright notices, and the license notice with all copies. Modified versions must be released under precisely the GFDL, use a distinct title, list the modifying authors plus at least five principal authors of the original if there are that many, preserve copyright notices, include an unaltered copy of the license, preserve or create a History section, preserve network locations for transparent copies where applicable, preserve invariant sections, preserve warranty disclaimers, and comply with any cover-text requirements.[^17]

Wikipedia's own terms soften the practical burden for Wikipedia reuse by authorizing attribution through a hyperlink or URL to the page reused, through a stable alternative copy with equivalent credit, or through a list of all authors. The Terms also require a licensing notice with a hyperlink/URL to the license text or a copy of the license, and they require modifications to be clearly indicated. That site-level attribution framework matters because it is what Wikimedia tells reusers to do when using Wikipedia text under the CC route.[^18]

For share-alike, the GFDL is stricter in form. If you create a modified version, that modified version must be licensed under the same GFDL license, with the modified version filling the role of the original document. Under CC BY-SA 4.0, share-alike applies only if you share Adapted Material. The adapter's license must be BY-SA 4.0 or later, a ported 4.0+ version if any, or another license that Creative Commons has designated as BY-SA compatible. Mere inclusion in a collection does not automatically force the entire collection under share-alike.[^19]

The GFDL's distinctive legacy features are invariant sections, cover texts, and the transparent/opaque copy framework. Invariant sections must be "Secondary Sections" and cannot cover the substantive topic itself. Front-cover texts are capped at 5 words and back-cover texts at 25 words. If more than 100 opaque copies are distributed, the distributor must either include a transparent copy or provide a network location where the public may download one, and keep it available for at least one year after the last distribution of that edition. These are alien to CC BY-SA 4.0, which instead focuses on attribution, notice preservation, and no additional restrictions.[^20]

For termination, both licenses terminate automatically upon breach, but the cure mechanics differ. CC BY-SA 4.0 reinstates rights automatically if the violation is cured within 30 days of discovery. CC BY-SA 3.0 had no comparable automatic cure. GFDL 1.3 also introduced cure and reinstatement language: rights are reinstated provisionally when the violation ceases, become permanent absent timely notice, and also become permanent on a first notice cured within 30 days of receipt.[^21]

For jurisdiction and international scope, CC BY-SA 4.0 was drafted as an international license rather than a ported set of national licenses, and Creative Commons describes it as more globally robust and supports official translations of the same legal text. By contrast, CC BY-SA 3.0 and earlier had jurisdiction-specific ports, some with local-law or forum-selection variations. The GFDL, meanwhile, grants a world-wide license in a single common text and does not rely on a porting system.[^22]

A final major difference is database rights. CC BY-SA 4.0 expressly covers sui generis database rights, and Wikimedia's Terms of Use add a waiver of such rights where contributors own them; Wikimedia's legal note explicitly says this helps keep reuse consistent internationally. Neither GFDL-era Wikimedia practice nor CC BY-SA 3.0 handled database rights as cleanly. For ordinary Wikipedia prose, this matters mostly at the margins, but it is one reason Wikimedia preferred the 4.0 upgrade.[^23]

## Compatibility and migration paths

The legal bridges between these licenses are asymmetric. The crucial historical bridge from GFDL to CC BY-SA was not a general compatibility rule. It was a one-time, tightly limited relicensing mechanism added in GFDL 1.3 section 11 for qualifying wiki-style collaborative works. The GNU FAQ explains that qualifying material had to be under GFDL 1.3 or "1.2 or later," had to have no cover texts or invariant sections, and outside-origin material had to be incorporated into the wiki before November 1, 2008; the permission to republish under CC BY-SA on the same site ended before August 1, 2009. That is the legal reason Wikipedia could move to the CC framework in 2009, not a standing general compatibility principle between the two licenses.[^12]

The Wikimedia side of that migration was also explicitly dual rather than fully substitutive. Wikimedia's 2009 Q&A said that existing content available under GFDL would also be made available under CC BY-SA, and future revisions would be dual licensed, except where external CC BY-SA-only additions from outside sources were incorporated. The same materials make clear that reusers—not editors—would bear the burden of determining whether an article remained GFDL-available.[^33]

That remains important today. Wikimedia's current Terms of Use say reusers may comply with either license or both, but they also say that if text was imported from another source under a compatible CC BY-SA license but not GFDL, the reuser must comply with that CC-compatible license and does not have the option to relicense it under GFDL. Wikimedia therefore instructs reusers to check the page footer, page history, and discussion page to determine what license applies to the text they want to reuse and whether additional visible attribution notices must be preserved.[^34]

The migration from CC BY-SA 3.0 to CC BY-SA 4.0 is much cleaner. Creative Commons' 3.0 license allows an adaptation to be distributed under a later version of the same license, and Wikimedia's 2016 legal note relied on exactly that clause in explaining how existing 3.0 Wikipedia text could move to 4.0 in the collaborative editing process. That makes 3.0 → 4.0 a normal later-version path, not a special relicensing exception like GFDL section 11.[^35]

For current reuse analysis, that yields a practical matrix:

| From | To | Status | Why |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| GFDL-only collaborative wiki text that met section 11 conditions | CC BY-SA 3.0 | Historically permitted during the 2008–2009 window | GFDL 1.3 section 11 |
| CC BY-SA 3.0 text | CC BY-SA 4.0 | Permitted via later-version mechanism | 3.0 share-alike clause and Wikimedia legal note |
| CC BY-SA-compatible imports not also GFDL | GFDL | Not available on Wikimedia's terms | Terms of Use expressly deny the GFDL option for such text |
| Solely GFDL external content | Import into Wikimedia text today | Not permitted | Terms of Use say you shall not import content available solely under GFDL |
| CC BY text | Incorporate into Wikipedia | Permitted if CC BY terms are observed | CC FAQ confirms CC BY may be incorporated into CC BY-SA works |

For CC BY-SA compatibility statements beyond Wikipedia, Creative Commons currently says that adaptations of BY-SA 4.0 material may be licensed under BY-SA 4.0 or later, ported 4.0+ versions if any, or a BY-SA Compatible License on CC's approved list. Creative Commons currently lists the Free Art License 1.3 and GPLv3 as such compatible licenses for 4.0, with GPLv3 compatibility expressly described as one-way. For BY-SA 3.0, CC says later versions of BY-SA are allowed, but no non-CC licenses have been designated compatible. Those compatibility statements matter mostly for sophisticated remixing contexts, not ordinary Wikipedia article reuse.[^36]

## Practical compliance for common reuse settings

For a normal website or web app that republishes Wikipedia text, the cleanest path is usually the CC BY-SA 4.0 route. Wikimedia's Terms of Use allow attribution through a hyperlink or URL to the reused page, to a stable alternative copy with equivalent credit, or through a list of all authors. The English Wikipedia reuse page also says each copy or modified version must include a licensing notice stating that the work is released under CC BY-SA and either a hyperlink/URL to the license text or a copy of the license.[^38]

A practical website attribution block can therefore look like this:

```text
<p> This page reuses text from the Wikipedia article "Metasyntactic variable," available under CC BY-SA 4.0. Changes were made. Source article and contributors: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metasyntactic_variable License: https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/4.0/ </p>
```

That structure tracks Wikimedia's accepted attribution methods and the CC BY-SA 4.0 requirement to retain attribution information, indicate modifications, and include the license text or link in a reasonable manner for the web context.[^39]

If your reuse takes place on another MediaWiki-based wiki, Wikimedia provides a dedicated attribution template pattern. The English Wikipedia template {{En-WP attribution notice|Page name}} is expressly intended to provide attribution from external wikis and renders a notice stating that the article uses material from a named Wikipedia article released under the CC BY-SA 4.0 International License, with a "view authors" link.[^40]

For a book, magazine, or offline PDF, the same legal elements apply, but the medium changes the implementation. Under Wikimedia's Terms, you may satisfy attribution by linking to the reused page or to a stable equivalent copy, or by providing a list of authors. English Wikipedia's reuse page also states that a reuser may distribute a copy together with a copy of the CC BY-SA 4.0 license and list at least five principal authors on the title page or top of the document.[^38]

A conservative book-style notice might therefore read:

```text
Portions of this chapter are adapted from the Wikipedia article "Metasyntactic variable" and are licensed under Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike 4.0 International. Source page and contributors: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metasyntactic_variable License text: https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/4.0/ Changes were made for length and style.
```

If the publication is offline-only, include either the full CC BY-SA 4.0 text in the back matter or a durable URL plus a way for readers to access the source article and contributors. That maps directly onto Wikimedia's "hyperlink/URL or copy of the license" approach and CC's "reasonable manner" attribution rule.[^41]

For software documentation, the legal analysis should separate the documentation from the software code. Creative Commons expressly says it does not recommend CC licenses for software itself, but they may be used for software documentation and separate artistic elements. If you adapt Wikipedia text into your manuals, help pages, or in-product docs, the documentation portion should follow CC BY-SA 4.0, including attribution and share-alike for the adapted text. That does not automatically convert your source code into CC BY-SA merely because the product ships with separately attributed documentation, so long as you are dealing with a documentation layer or another collection rather than a license-confused remix of code and text.[^42]

For commercial use, neither license blocks it. Wikimedia's Terms of Use explicitly say the text licenses "do allow commercial uses" so long as the terms are met, and Creative Commons' official summary of BY-SA 4.0 says reuse is allowed "for any purpose, even commercially." Commercial publishers, AI vendors, SaaS tools, textbook companies, and documentation vendors therefore usually do not need additional permission from Wikimedia or article authors; they need compliance, especially attribution and share-alike where adaptation is shared.[^43]

If you intentionally choose the GFDL path for reuse, the compliance burden becomes materially higher. A verbatim copy must reproduce the license and copyright notices. A derivative version must remain under the GFDL, acknowledge authors as specified, and provide access to a transparent copy. Wikipedia's reuse page specifically points GFDL reusers to section 2 for verbatim copies and says derivative reuse entails GFDL licensing, acknowledgment of article authorship, and access to the transparent copy of the material. In practice, that is why the CC path is usually preferred for ordinary websites and books.[^44]

## Risks and edge cases

Translations are adaptations under both licensing systems. The GFDL defines a modified version to include a work translated into another language, and CC BY-SA 4.0 defines adapted material to include material in which the licensed material is translated, altered, arranged, transformed, or otherwise modified in a permission-requiring way. So a translation of Wikipedia prose is not mere copying; it triggers the adaptation rules, including change indication and share-alike under the CC route.[^45]

Excerpts do not automatically escape the licenses merely because they are short. If the excerpt is substantial enough to require permission under copyright law, the license terms still apply. At the same time, CC's FAQ is explicit that license terms do not apply where an exception or limitation such as fair use or fair dealing already authorizes the use. So the real threshold question is not length alone; it is whether copyright permission is needed in the jurisdiction and context.[^46]

Mixed-license article histories are the most important Wikipedia-specific edge case. Wikimedia's Terms of Use warn that some text may have been imported from another source under a compatible CC BY-SA license but not GFDL, and that such text may also have extra attribution requirements. Wikimedia instructs reusers to review the page footer, history, and discussion page, and says visible notices or banners identifying originally external publication should be preserved. This means that "Wikipedia text" is not always a uniform relicensable blob: the article-level attribution trail matters.[^18]

Media files are a separate licensing universe. Wikipedia's text is generally reusable under the text licenses, but individual images, sounds, and videos often have their own licenses, and the Wikipedia reuse page stresses that each media file has its own information page and licensing statement. Some files are under different free licenses; some are public domain; and some are non-free fair-use files, which usually cannot be reused outside the exact lawful context that justifies them on Wikipedia. A reuser should therefore never assume that "the whole article" is under one uniform license if it includes media.[^47]

Templates and transclusions can create practical confusion even when they do not create a novel licensing rule. If you copy rendered article output, you may also reproduce text coming from templates or modules and media coming from separate file pages. The safest course is to treat the page you copied from as the attribution anchor for text, while separately inspecting every reused media file and any visible imported-content notice. That recommendation follows Wikimedia's instruction to consult the page history/talk/footer for text provenance and to examine file description pages for media provenance.[^48]

A subtler risk is stale guidance. Some Wikipedia project pages still reflect the CC BY-SA 3.0 era and have not been updated to today's 4.0-centered wording. If you rely on an old help page or third-party blog post instead of current Terms of Use and current license texts, you can end up with the wrong attribution template, the wrong license version, or the wrong understanding of GFDL availability.[^10]

## Recommended best practices and operational checklist

The single best practice is to treat CC BY-SA 4.0 as the default compliance path for current Wikipedia text reuse unless you have a very specific reason to rely on the GFDL. That recommendation follows both the current Terms of Use and the operational differences between the licenses: CC BY-SA 4.0 is flexible, internationally robust, and easier to satisfy in web, print, and software-documentation settings, whereas GFDL compliance is formal and manual-oriented.[^49]

A practical checklist for reusing Wikipedia text is:

- Confirm that you are reusing text, not assuming that embedded media follows the same license. Check every file page separately.[^47]

- Check whether your use is already authorized by fair use, fair dealing, or another exception/limitation; if so, the CC terms may not be triggered.[^46]

- Review the page footer, page history, and talk page for imported text, CC-only material, or extra attribution notices. Preserve visible attribution banners or notations.[^18]

- For the CC BY-SA 4.0 route, include: source-article identification, attribution to contributors through an accepted Wikimedia method, a clear statement that the text is under CC BY-SA 4.0, and either a copy of the license or a license link. If you modified or translated the text, say so.[^50]

- If your reuse is a translation, adaptation, abridgment, or edited version, apply CC BY-SA 4.0 or later to your contributed adapted text.[^51]

- Use the GFDL route only if you have confirmed that the article remains GFDL-available and you are prepared to satisfy the heavier documentary formalities, including license-copy and transparent-copy obligations where relevant.[^52]

- For commercial use, focus on compliance rather than permission-seeking; the licenses allow commercial reuse.[^43]

The bottom-line legal picture is therefore this: Wikipedia text reuse is broad and powerful, but not frictionless. The current Wikimedia framework strongly supports reuse, including commercial reuse, yet it expects reusers to respect provenance, attribution, modification marking, and the possibility that some article text is available only through the CC path and not the GFDL path. For modern applications, a well-documented CC BY-SA 4.0 workflow is the safest default.[^53]
