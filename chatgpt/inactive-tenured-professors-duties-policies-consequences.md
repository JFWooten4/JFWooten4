# Inactive Tenured Professors: Duties, Policies, and Consequences

Executive Summary. Academic tenure guarantees job security and protects academic freedom, but it does not excuse total inaction. Tenured professors still have contractual duties (teaching, research, service) and may face discipline for "neglect of duty," incompetence, or misconduct. Institutions typically address inactivity first through evaluation and remediation (performance plans, counseling, warnings) and escalate to formal proceedings only if problems persist. In due process terms, public‐college faculty have constitutional protections (notice and a hearing) before dismissal,[^1] while private institutions rely on faculty handbooks or contracts.[^2][^3] Rarely, chronic neglect leads to suspension or termination, often following intensive review and appeal. The process is usually lengthy (performance reviews → informal correction → formal notice → hearing → governing board decision).[^4][^5] For example, Temple University (2002) terminated a tenured math professor for "neglect of duty and incompetence" after faculty committees recommended discipline.[^6][^7] Likewise, Princeton (2021) removed a tenured architecture professor for repeatedly refusing teaching/advising duties – citing "substantial and manifest neglect of duty" and policy violations.[^8] We found that almost every university handbook allows discipline for neglect, and states often legislate periodic reviews or additional scrutiny of tenured faculty. Where enforcement fails, departments bear reputational and educational costs (students complain, morale suffers).

Purpose of Tenure. Academic tenure is designed to protect academic freedom and institutional integrity, not to shelter laziness.[^9][^10] As a BestColleges overview notes, tenure emerged to "insulate" professors from dismissal over unpopular views, enabling free inquiry.[^9] It also attracts scholarly talent by trading long-term job security for modest salaries.[^11] However, as Rice University explains, tenure is granted only to those with "capacity for a lifetime of scholarship, teaching, and service," and it "does not protect demonstrated incompetence in teaching and research, [or] substantial and manifest neglect of duty".[^10] Tenured faculty retain all usual obligations: teaching courses, advising students, publishing or professional activity, and serving on committees. (For example, Rice’s handbook expects faculty to be "reasonably available" to students and participate in campus life.[^12][^13]) In return, both public and private universities promise for-cause dismissal only, usually citing AAUP-endorsed grounds: serious professional misconduct, incompetence, or neglect of duty.[^2]

Faculty Duties and Institutional Policies. Nearly all faculty handbooks and policies enumerate the core duties of tenured professors (teaching, research, service) and state that these must be met. For instance, the University of Michigan’s faculty code obliges professors to "maintain high standards of teaching, scholarship, research, service, and professional conduct," and explicitly allows dismissal for "demonstrated misconduct in teaching or research, substantial and manifest neglect of duty".[^14] Similarly, Stanford’s discipline policy (private) requires high professional conduct and lists "neglect of University-related duties" as actionable misconduct.[^15] Universities often define "neglect of duty" to include, for example, failing to teach assigned courses, refusing committee service, abandoning students, or missing required evaluations. Florida Atlantic University’s (public) policy even treats 3+ days of unauthorized absence as job abandonment leading to termination.[^16][^17] In practice, presidents, deans, and faculty senates rely on detailed faculty handbooks for the formal procedure. These usually mirror AAUP guidelines: dismissal can be for "incompetence or dishonesty in teaching or research, substantial and manifest neglect of duty, [or] personal conduct impairing responsibilities".[^2] Many handbooks (private and public) echo Rice and Michigan: tenure "does not protect" poor performance.[^10][^14]

Typical Administrative Response (Discipline Timeline). When inactivity is detected (often via student complaints, peer review, or annual evaluations), administrators generally start with informal corrective steps. These may include a performance improvement plan (PIP), counseling, teaching support, or reduced duties. For example, University of Texas System rules envision that "unsatisfactory" performance triggers mentoring or remediation.[^5] If no improvement occurs, the faculty member is given formal notice of deficiencies. This can escalate to a formal disciplinary process. Figure 1 below shows a typical sequence:

```text
flowchart LR A[Issue Identified: Poor performance] --> B{Informal steps taken?} B -->|Yes| C[Performance meeting or warning] C --> D{Improved?} D -->|Yes| E[Monitor; return to normal evaluation] D -->|No| F[Formal notice of unsatisfactory performance] B -->|No| F F --> G[Investigation & charges (e.g., neglect of duty)] G --> H[Hearing or review panel] H --> I{Findings of "cause"?} I -->|No cause| J[Case closed; possibly monitor] I -->|Cause found| K[Admin recommended action] K --> L{Recommended sanction} L -->|Lesser (warning, PIP, or pay cut)| M[Implement sanction (Provost/President)] L -->|Dismissal recommended| N[President forwards case to Board] N --> O{Board vote} O -->|Dismissal approved| P[Faculty dismissed] O -->|No dismissal (or closed)| M
```

Figure 1. Typical process flow when a tenured professor’s performance triggers discipline. Initial steps may include warnings or remediation. If problems persist, formal charges lead to a hearing. If cause is found, sanctions (up to dismissal) are recommended. In many institutions, a dismissal recommendation must go to the governing board.[^4][^18]

Once a formal charge is filed, universities follow their due‐process rules. At Stanford (private), for example, the Provost investigates and may officially charge a professor, who is then notified and allowed to respond; if unresolved, the Advisory Board (faculty committee) holds a hearing.[^19] Michigan State (public) uses a hearing committee, then an appeal panel of tenured peers (under a faculty union).[^20][^18] Under MSU’s policy, if the hearing or president recommends dismissal, the Board of Trustees decides; if only lesser discipline is warranted, the president imposes it.[^4][^18] In all cases, tenure systems require proof by clear and convincing evidence of "cause" (incompetence or neglect) before firing a tenured professor.[^21][^4] If evidence is insufficient, the professor is exonerated and retains tenure.

Due Process and Grievance Rights. Tenured faculty generally have substantial procedural protections. In public universities, tenure is recognized as a property interest, so the Fourteenth Amendment guarantees notice and a fair hearing before termination.[^1][^3] (For example, in Barazi v. WV State College (WV 1997), the court affirmed termination for cause but penalized the college with nominal damages for denying a pre-termination hearing.[^1]) In private institutions, tenure is enforced via contract law: faculty handbooks are construed as part of the employment contract.[^22][^3] Thus, failing to follow prescribed procedures (e.g. a promised faculty tribunal) can lead to breach-of-contract suits. In either case, universities must adhere to their own policies. Most procedures allow the professor to have legal counsel or an advisor (Weingarten rights) and to file grievances at each stage.[^23][^24] Indeed, at MSU (where professors unionized in 2025), union contracts explicitly guarantee representation in discipline hearings,[^25] and in Michigan any faculty member may grieve a disciplinary decision through a formal grievance process.[^26]

In unionized campuses, collective bargaining defines extra protections (notice periods, appeal panels). In other countries (e.g. UK), faculty typically have permanent contracts but may be terminated via "redundancy" or misconduct processes under employment law.

Representative Case Studies. Real-world examples illustrate how theory plays out:

- Tenured Professor Fired for Neglect (Temple U., 2002): Temple University’s president announced the firing of a tenured math professor "guilty of neglect of duty and incompetence in meeting his teaching responsibilities".[^6] After 30+ years on faculty, the professor had refused to inflate grades as requested; Temple had faculty committees review the case, which unanimously recommended discipline. In an email to faculty, the president acknowledged removing tenure was "one of the gravest matters" but necessary to uphold standards.[^7] Faculty leaders (senate and union) supported the process, noting accountability for teaching had increased over decades.[^32]

- Princeton Architecture Dean (2021): Alejandro Zaera-Polo, a tenured architecture professor and former dean at Princeton, was investigated and eventually dismissed for cause. The Dean of the Faculty found that Zaera-Polo had "repeatedly" violated university policies and "substantially and manifestly neglected" his duties (e.g. refusing to advise thesis students).[^8] After multiple warnings and an administrative leave, Princeton’s Provost and then Board of Trustees approved his dismissal. Zaera-Polo appealed to Princeton’s faculty committee (CCFA), but it unanimously upheld the dismissal after finding he "persisted in misconduct" that harmed students.[^33][^34] Ultimately the Trustees affirmed termination.[^35] The case underscores that even prominent tenured faculty can be removed for dereliction of academic duty.

- Neglect of Duty Confirmed (WV State College, 1997): In Barazi v. West Virginia State College, a tenured professor had been teaching only night classes while working full-time elsewhere, neglecting his day-time duties. The college reprimanded him for failing to hold office hours, file syllabi, etc., and listed nine failures in a termination letter.[^3] A hearing officer found a "continual… pattern… which constitutes neglect of duty"[^3] and the college’s board upheld dismissal. The WV Supreme Court agreed termination was for cause, though it ordered only nominal damages for a due-process lapse (no back pay).[^1] This case shows that blatant abandonment of teaching obligations can defeat tenure protections.

- Performance Remediation (University of Texas System): Texas law requires periodic reviews of tenured faculty. The UT System rule mandates that an unsatisfactory review leads to a written development plan or remediation (e.g. teaching training).[^5] Only if "incompetence, neglect of duty, or other good cause" is confirmed does the process move to formal discipline.[^30] This illustrates a structured approach: support and improvement first, only then sanctions.

Administrative Remedies & Union Role. Before reaching a firing decision, universities often try alternatives: reassignment, reduced load, leave, or performance plans. For example, a college might relieve a professor of teaching heavy courses, give a lighter advising load, or offer sabbatical if problems stem from burnout. Many institutions use an unsatisfactory review to trigger remediation (e.g. mentorship, pedagogical training).[^5] In some cases, a professor may be placed on paid administrative leave during investigation (with pay, pending outcome).[^27] If discipline is warranted but dismissal too severe, universities may impose lesser penalties: formal reprimands, salary reductions, unpaid suspensions (often up to 6 months).[^4][^29]

Where faculty are unionized, collective bargaining adds layers. Union contracts often detail discipline procedures (notice periods, representation rights) and prevent managers from bypassing agreed processes. For instance, Michigan State’s faculty union guarantees the right to a hearing and to have a union representative in disciplinary proceedings.[^23][^26] Unions can also negotiate outcomes like generous severance if a tenured post is eliminated, or arbitration if disputes arise. Conversely, administrators may argue that longstanding tenure rules supersede union layoff provisions, leading to complex legal arguments.

Reputational and Financial Impacts. An inactive tenured professor can harm an institution in subtle ways. Quality of education suffers (students complain of unavailable or uninspired instructors). Departments with "deadwood" faculty may struggle to obtain research grants or meet accreditation standards. Media and lawmakers sometimes seize on anecdotes of "professors bragging about working little" to justify tenure reforms.[^36] For example, Nebraska legislators cited such stories when proposing to abolish tenure for new hires.[^36] Additionally, litigation costs (if procedures are mishandled) and faculty turnover (if morale drops) can strain budgets. Universities thus have an incentive to address neglect promptly, balancing the high bar for firing tenured professors against the costs of inaction.

Recommendations for Stakeholders.

- Administrators: Follow established policies scrupulously. Document all performance issues, provide clear evaluations, and offer support (mentoring, development plans) early. Use progressive discipline: issue warnings, require improvement plans, and only escalate to formal proceedings when problems are persistent. Consult legal counsel to ensure due process, especially if public employees. Engage faculty governance bodies (senates, promotion committees) when launching a dismissal case.[^7] If dismissal is pursued, prepare thorough evidence of "cause" (as in Barazi[^3]). Coordinate with HR and, if applicable, union reps to avoid procedural missteps. Communicate carefully to faculty and students, while maintaining confidentiality, to manage reputational risk.

- Faculty Colleagues: Promote a culture of accountability. If a peer is not fulfilling duties, department chairs or colleagues should document concerns (attendance, quality of teaching, research output) and report them through the proper channels. Encourage the struggling professor to improve (offer peer mentoring). Resist covering for a negligent colleague by taking on their committee or advising burdens without formal adjustment. If the university initiates action, testify or provide information honestly for any review, as happened at Temple.[^32] Faculty senates and unions should ensure that any process is fair: they can negotiate clear standards for "neglect of duty" in contracts, or insist on faculty representation on hearing committees.

- Students: Students are often first to notice a truly inactive professor (unprepared lectures, unavailable office hours). They should voice concerns via course evaluations and to department leaders or ombudspersons. Many universities allow confidential student feedback to be shared with administrators. If a professor has effectively disappeared from their duties, students can petition the department or academic affairs office for intervention (e.g. reassignment of classes or an alternate instructor). In extreme cases (such as a professor on unauthorized leave), students may collectively file a grievance or alert a student ombuds. Supporting documentation (dates of missed classes, copies of unanswered emails) strengthens the case. While tenure protects faculty jobs, students still have rights to an education; administrators can prioritize student welfare by addressing faculty neglect.

In summary, tenure is not a license to do nothing. Universities maintain detailed codes spelling out faculty duties and the consequences of dereliction (see, e.g., Rice,[^10] Stanford,[^15] FAU[^16]). When inactivity occurs, the typical path is: review and remediation → formal charge → hearing and appeal → possible sanction or dismissal.[^5][^4] Across public/private and research/teaching institutions, the outcomes vary (from warning to termination), but the principles hold: tenure is a right with responsibility. Careful adherence to process ensures universities can act against genuine neglect without undermining tenure’s core purpose.
