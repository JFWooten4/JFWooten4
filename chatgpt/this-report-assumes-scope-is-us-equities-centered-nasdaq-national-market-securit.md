# Mandatory Market Making in the Nasdaq SOES Era and Its Legacy

## Executive summary

This report assumes the scope is U.S. equities, centered on Nasdaq National Market securities and the SOES-centered dealer structure that emerged in the late 1980s. In this context, "mandatory market making" means the package of obligations under which Nasdaq/NMS market makers had to participate in SOES, honor displayed quotations under firm-quote rules, and maintain minimum displayed proprietary size in a market whose public quote-setting power still sat mainly with dealers. I use "post-mandatory" as shorthand for the post-1996/1997 hybrid regime created by the SEC’s Order Handling Rules and related Nasdaq reforms, which substantially weakened the old dealer-only mandatory structure even though quote-honoring obligations did not disappear.[^1]

The central finding is that SOES-era mandatory participation solved a real access problem, but it was not a durable market-design solution. After the October 1987 market break, the NASD and SEC wanted small investors to have dependable automated access to dealers even when human negotiation failed; mandatory SOES participation and tighter withdrawal penalties were the institutional answer. That did improve immediacy and guaranteed access for small orders. But because the surrounding market structure still let dealers dominate public quotes, hide better prices in private systems, and influence self-regulation, the mandate also intensified stale-quote risk, adverse-selection costs, and resistance by incumbents to competitive quoting.[^2]

Economically, the evidence is mixed on whether the mandate itself increased or decreased overall liquidity, but it is much clearer on who bore the burden and how the burden was redistributed. Before the reforms of the late 1990s, Nasdaq execution costs for many trades were materially higher than on the NYSE, quote clustering kept public spreads artificially wide, and the SEC found that many investors could not reliably transact at the best available prices. At the same time, some studies and regulatory reviews suggested SOES trading forced faster quote updates and improved price discovery, especially over somewhat longer intraday intervals. The most defensible reading is that mandatory market making improved access and continuity, but did so in a way that preserved dealer rents and exported the resulting tensions into spreads, quote behavior, and inter-firm conflict.[^3]

The industry’s eventual answer was not to rely on mandatory dealer participation alone, but to move toward a more transparent, more order-driven, and more auditable market. The SEC’s 1996 order against the NASD, the 1996/1997 Order Handling Rules, OATS in 1998, and CAT under Rule 613 after 2012 all point in the same direction: independent governance, better customer and limit-order display, lower barriers to new liquidity suppliers, stronger enforcement of firm-quote and reporting duties, and complete order-lifecycle surveillance. In short, the legacy of mandatory market making was to show that quote obligations without governance independence, market transparency, and auditability produce predictable failure modes.[^4]

## Scope and analytical frame

The primary sources used here are the SEC’s 1996 Section 21(a) Report and Appendix on the NASD and Nasdaq, the SEC’s 1996 administrative order against the NASD, the SEC’s Order Handling Rules materials, the SEC’s 1998 OATS approval order, the SEC’s 2012 Rule 613 CAT adopting release, the 2016 CAT NMS Plan approval order, and FINRA’s 2021 OATS retirement notice. I supplement those sources with GAO’s 1998 SOES study, SEC staff studies on ECNs and after-hours trading, and leading academic papers on quote clustering, spreads, execution costs, and post-reform competition.[^5]

A key analytical distinction is between market access obligations and market quality outcomes. Mandatory participation in SOES guaranteed small-order access to displayed dealer quotes, but market quality also depended on whether those quotes were competitive, whether dealers could coordinate or retaliate, whether better prices were hidden in private systems, whether trade reports were timely and accurate, and whether regulators could reconstruct what happened. Much of the 1990s debate confused those different issues; the historical record is clearer if they are kept separate.[^6]

## Historical trajectory

Nasdaq’s SOES began as an automation project for small retail orders in a dealer market that still relied heavily on telephone negotiation and manual processes. GAO records that SOES was established in 1984 as a voluntary means for market makers to provide timely and efficient execution of small retail orders. The SEC’s 1984 materials likewise describe SOES as an order-routing and execution system for limited-size OTC orders, and contemporaneous NASD materials described it as a pioneering automatic execution system for small customer orders.[^7]

| --- |

The decisive break came after the October 1987 crash. GAO states that many market makers withdrew from SOES during the crisis and that small investors had difficulty executing orders; the NASD then made SOES participation mandatory for Nasdaq National Market securities. The SEC’s June 1988 materials confirm that participation as a SOES market maker became mandatory for all market makers in Nasdaq/NMS securities, and the 21(a) Appendix says that on June 30, 1988 SOES was changed to require all Nasdaq market makers to participate while tightening penalties for unexcused withdrawals.[^8]

The early 1990s exposed the downside of that solution. The SEC later found that Nasdaq dealers followed anticompetitive quoting conventions, retaliated against narrower spreads, coordinated quote and trade behavior, delayed trade reports, and failed to honor quotes; the NASD had evidence of these problems by at least the summer of 1990 and did not respond adequately. At the same time, SOES itself became controversial because the system designed for retail access was increasingly used by high-frequency day-trading firms—the GAO notes that 1995 NASD data showed SOES day-trading firms accounted for more than 80% of SOES volume.[^9]

|  |
| --- |

The 1996 SEC actions were the hinge point. In the Section 21(a) materials, the Commission laid out the record of quote clustering, retaliation, trade reporting failures, firm-quote failures, and supervisory breakdowns. In the administrative order, the SEC censured the NASD and required a remedial package that included at least 50% independent public/non-industry governance, autonomy for regulatory staff, uniform access standards, independent internal audit, stronger order-handling surveillance, better trade-reporting reliability, better firm-quote enforcement, and a marketwide audit trail sufficient to reconstruct the life of orders from first receipt to execution or non-execution. OATS and then CAT were direct descendants of that remedial logic.[^10]

## Economic and competitive effects

The strongest economic case for mandatory SOES participation is that it improved immediacy and access for small orders in a dealer market that had failed under stress. GAO says SOES provided users access to market makers during the high-volume periods that followed the 1987 crash, and the system’s automatic execution function reduced the dependence of small customer orders on dealer callback behavior. That benefit mattered: the underlying post-crash policy goal was not abstract efficiency, but preventing a repeat of a market in which retail orders became operationally stranded.[^11]

The strongest economic case against the regime is that it embedded those access benefits inside a public quote structure that was still systematically noncompetitive. The SEC’s Appendix found that more than 80% of domestic Nasdaq National Market stocks followed the even-eighth convention before late May 1994, and that this convention often kept the minimum inside spread at $1/4 rather than $1/8. The Commission concluded that investors in these stocks frequently faced wider public spreads and execution prices less favorable than they would have seen without the convention. Academic evidence before the reforms points the same way: Christie and Schultz found odd-eighth quotes were virtually nonexistent for 70 of 100 active Nasdaq stocks, and Huang and Stoll found Nasdaq effective spreads were more than twice NYSE effective spreads overall, with especially large differences for small trades.[^12]

That combination helps explain why mandatory participation produced such sharp conflict between access and dealer economics. GAO reports that market makers said compulsory SOES exposure raised their costs because stale quotes became easier to hit, because automatic executions let day traders trade ahead of slower dealer quote updates, and because dealers could be forced to trade with better-informed counterparties. GAO also notes unpublished NASD and academic work suggesting that stocks with heavier SOES activity experienced larger declines in market-maker participation and that adverse selection from SOES trading may have contributed to wider spreads. This does not prove the mandate alone widened spreads, but it does show that the mandate changed the economics of quoting by making slow quote maintenance more expensive.[^13]

The evidence on liquidity and depth is accordingly mixed. Dealers told GAO they reduced the number of stocks in which they made markets and supplied less capital because enforced exposure to SOES traders made stale quotes costly; day-trading firms and some other participants replied that day-trader activity itself added liquidity and forced better quote maintenance. GAO’s own bottom line was cautious: the studies it reviewed often could not isolate SOES from concurrent rule changes, profitability shifts, and broader market conditions. That caution is important. The most rigorous conclusion is not that mandatory market making simply "destroyed" liquidity, but that it reallocated liquidity provision from a protected dealer margin model toward a more contested and automation-sensitive model.[^14]

The evidence on volatility is also nuanced rather than one-sided. Battalio, Hatch, and Jennings found that over one-minute intervals high levels of maximum-sized SOES trades were associated with higher volatility, but over five-minute intervals intense maximum-sized SOES trading was associated with lower volatility; they interpreted that pattern, together with Harris and Schultz, as consistent with more efficient price discovery rather than a case for eliminating SOES on volatility grounds. GAO summarized the literature similarly: some studies found short-run increases in volatility, but others found that SOES trading moved prices to equilibrium faster.[^15]

On execution quality, the decisive shift came after the SEC’s Order Handling Rules. The SEC later explained that before those rules, market makers could place better prices in private ECNs than in the public market, producing a two-tier market with artificially wide public quotes. After the Order Handling Rules required customer limit-order display and ECN price display, the SEC reported that spreads narrowed dramatically and cited NASD work finding quoted spreads fell by as much as 41%; GAO also reported post-rule spread declines of more than 35% in affected stocks and a fall in SOES share volume of about 6 percentage points. Weston’s work similarly found that the reforms reduced dealer rents and greatly diminished the spread gap between Nasdaq and the NYSE.[^16]

The competitive effects point in the same direction. The SEC found that groups of "friendly competitors" among market makers posed a significant obstacle to new entrants, and that attempts to obtain order flow by narrowing spreads could trigger harassment and refusals to trade. The SEC also later noted that the old 1,000-share minimum proprietary quote size was itself a barrier to entry for market making, and Nasdaq’s own 1998 filing argued that once customer and ECN orders were integrated into the public market, those mandatory size rules were no longer needed. In other words, the old mandatory regime did not just impose obligations; it also protected incumbents.[^17]

## Operational and technological consequences

Operationally, mandatory SOES participation forced much more of Nasdaq trading into a real-time automated channel before the surrounding infrastructure was fully ready for it. The SEC’s 1989 automation policy statement says the 1987 market break exposed capacity and contingency weaknesses across automated systems and notes that, in October 1989, the NASD experienced SOES problems. The Commission specifically observed that mandatory participation in SOES, tighter withdrawal penalties, and efforts to unlock and uncross markets generated an unprecedented flow of quote changes and apparently caused a communications delay between the computers running Nasdaq and SOES. That is an early example of a pattern that would recur for three decades: once firms are required to expose quotes electronically, systems engineering becomes market regulation.[^18]

The operational weaknesses the SEC identified in 1996 were not subtle. The 21(a) Appendix says late and inaccurate trade reporting distorted the appearance of the market and misled investors who relied on the tape; in the broker-dealer segment the Commission examined, late reports were severe enough to raise serious concern. The same Appendix says many order tickets were inaccurate, some lacked execution times, some were missing altogether, and timestamping was often unreliable for testing compliance with firm-quote and limit-order rules. In a decentralized dealer market, that meant the basic records needed to determine "what happened first" often did not exist in usable form.[^19]

Those failures directly shaped the design of OATS. In 1998 the SEC approved OATS to create an accurate, time-sequenced record of Nasdaq orders and related events, requiring firms to electronically record and report orders that were originated, received, transmitted, modified, canceled, or executed. The SEC’s OATS approval order explicitly linked the system to the abuses described in the 21(a) Report and said the benefits to surveillance and enforcement outweighed the systems costs imposed on members. In practical industry terms, OATS was a forced integration layer between trading desks, order-management workflows, and the regulator’s reconstruction needs.[^20]

CAT extended that logic from Nasdaq-centric order audit to a cross-market lifecycle model. In Rule 613, the SEC said existing audit trails were not efficient or adequate because they did not capture away-from-inside quotes and orders, did not identify participants below the clearing-broker level, lacked mandatory validated fields, and failed to connect activity to the customer in a sufficiently reliable way. The adopted rule therefore required synchronized business clocks, a unique customer identifier concept, reporting of comprehensive order-lifecycle data, and inclusion of all NMS securities in one audit trail. The SEC also made clear that regulators needed principal orders and market-maker quotations in CAT because effective surveillance of trading-ahead, spoofing, and layering depends on seeing the exact sequence between customer orders and proprietary activity.[^21]

CAT’s operational architecture also answers a practical OMS/EMS question that modern firms still face: at the source or at the repository? Rule 613 expressly allows data either to be reported in a uniform electronic format or in a manner that lets the central repository convert it into that format. The Commission said that flexibility was meant to avoid forcing broker-dealers and SROs to make unnecessary substantial changes to their order-management and execution systems, while still requiring the repository ultimately to store consolidated data in a uniform format. So CAT did not require all normalization to happen at the source; it permitted normalization either at the broker-dealer level or at the repository, as long as regulators received accurate, linkable audit data.[^22]

## Governance and regulatory consequences

The governance lesson of the SOES era is at least as important as the trading lesson. The 21(a) materials show that the SEC did not view Nasdaq’s failures as random enforcement mistakes; it saw them as a product of member influence over self-regulation. The Appendix records that seven of nine committee members at a 1990 Trading Committee meeting discussing spread issues were representatives of Nasdaq market-making firms, that a 1991 Quality of Markets Subcommittee on spreads was composed only of market-making firm representatives, and that 36 of 39 members of the Market Surveillance Committee during the relevant period worked for market-making firms.[^23]

The SEC’s later self-regulation concept release distilled that history bluntly. Looking back at the 21(a) Report, the Commission said the case raised concerns about inherent SRO conflicts, especially the lack of independence of NASD regulatory staff from Nasdaq’s market operations. The same release says the Commission found that Nasdaq market makers had exerted substantial influence over the affairs of the NASD through their dominant role in governance, in the disciplinary process, and in the operation of Nasdaq, while SOES-heavy firms and investor constituencies lacked comparable representation.[^24]

That governance imbalance had concrete regulatory effects. The SEC found that the NASD focused aggressively on SOES firms—using lists, sweep exams, and even emergency remedial proceedings—while being much less aggressive toward market-makers’ own rule violations. The Appendix says market makers pressed SOES rule changes through lobbying, majority participation in NASD committees, and in some cases influence with NASD staff; it also says the NASD pursued spread-narrowing measures that would be acceptable to the market-making community and publicly accepted the claim that SOES trading was the primary cause of wide spreads. This is the regulatory-capture core of the story: mandatory dealer obligations did not merely burden dealers; they also gave powerful dealers a reason to reshape rules and enforcement in their favor.[^25]

The SEC’s 1996 remedy was therefore structural, not cosmetic. In the administrative order, the Commission required at least 50% public/non-industry governance on key boards and committees, primary day-to-day authority for NASD Regulation, autonomy and insulation for regulatory staff, consistent access standards, a substantial independent internal-audit function reporting to a public-led audit committee, professional hearing officers, stronger order-handling and firm-quote surveillance, and an independent consultant. The order also required express rules against coordinated quotes, trades, and trade reports, as well as retaliation for competitive market behavior.[^26]

The legacy of that governance logic continued into the ECN and ATS era. In the 2004 SRO concept release, the SEC warned that an SRO might over-regulate members that operate competing markets and noted that these concerns were among the reasons it required the NASD to establish the Alternative Display Facility before permitting Nasdaq’s SuperMontage launch. That is a direct continuation of the 1996 lesson: when a venue is both regulator and competitor, market structure disputes cannot be treated as neutral engineering decisions.[^27]

## Regime comparison and bottom-line assessment

The table below compares the three regimes used in this report. Because there is no single formal "repeal date" for every SOES-related obligation, the third column should be read as the post-1997 hybrid order-driven regime ushered in by the Order Handling Rules, Actual Size reforms, ATS integration, and later OATS/CAT surveillance.[^28]

| Metric | Pre-mandatory regime | Mandatory SOES regime | Post-mandatory hybrid regime | Main evidence |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Approximate period | 1984 to mid-1988 | mid-1988 to 1996/1997 reform window | 1997 onward | SOES began as voluntary in 1984; participation became mandatory on June 30, 1988; Order Handling Rules phased in during 1997.[^29] |
| Core market design | Voluntary small-order auto-execution inside a dealer market | Mandatory SOES participation, mandatory displayed exposure, dealer-dominated public quotes | Hybrid market with customer limit orders and ECN prices entering the public quote |[^30] |
| Access for small orders | Better than pure telephone negotiation, but not guaranteed under stress | Stronger immediacy and dependable access to displayed dealer quotes | Access shifts from dealer obligation alone to displayed public order competition and ECN access |[^31] |
| Spreads and execution quality | Dealer market already associated with high execution costs | Public quotes often remained artificially wide because of even-eighth quoting and private inside-spread trading | Spreads narrowed dramatically after OHR; dealer rents fell and Nasdaq/NYSE spread gap diminished |[^32] |
| Depth and liquidity | Dealer capital central to public liquidity | Mixed: some evidence of reduced market-maker participation and dealer coverage, but not cleanly isolated | Actual-size studies found no material degradation in spread, volatility, depth, or liquidity from removing mandatory proprietary minimum size in the new hybrid regime |[^33] |
| Volatility | More manual, slower adjustment processes | Mixed: short-run volatility can rise with intense SOES trading, but longer-run price discovery can improve | More transparent and multi-venue competition; no persuasive evidence of material volatility increase from OHR/actual-size changes |[^34] |
| Competitive structure | Dealer-centric | Vulnerable to coordination, retaliation, and barriers to entry | More contestable: ECNs/ATSs become direct public competitors on quotes, execution times, and cost |[^35] |
| Surveillance and records | Fragmented desk records and limited automation | Still weak: late trade reports, missing order tickets, weak firm-quote enforcement | OATS and CAT move to electronic, time-sequenced, lifecycle reconstruction with synchronized clocks and broader coverage |[^36] |
| Governance incentives | Traditional SRO model | High risk of member influence over rules and enforcement | Greater formal separation and independence, though conflict never disappears entirely |[^37] |

The bottom-line assessment is that mandatory market making did two very different things at once. It improved access by forcing dealers to stand behind public quotes when the market most needed automatic execution. But it also made the cost of stale quoting and enforced exposure visible, which encouraged dealers to defend margins through spread rigidity, private price improvement in nonpublic systems, pressure for rule changes, and, in the SEC’s telling, sometimes coordination or retaliation. The mandate therefore revealed—not solved—the deeper design problem of a dealer market whose public prices were not yet genuinely competitive or fully auditable.[^38]

The industry’s durable response was a different architecture: public display of customer and ECN prices, lower entry barriers to liquidity provision, independent or more insulated regulation, and end-to-end auditability. That is why the straight historical line from SOES runs not only to day trading and ECNs, but also to OATS and CAT. Mandatory market making mattered less because it was the final answer than because it forced the market to learn what the final answer had to include.[^39]

## Open questions and limitations

The biggest empirical limitation is causal identification. GAO emphasized that most studies could not cleanly isolate the effects of SOES or SOES day trading from overlapping rule changes, profitability shifts, market conditions, and the later 1996/1997 reforms. That means any claim that mandatory participation alone widened spreads, reduced liquidity, or increased volatility should be treated cautiously. The strongest conclusions here are therefore the ones with the most direct documentary support: the mandate improved small-order access; dealers perceived higher stale-quote and adverse-selection costs; the public quote structure remained noncompetitive; and post-1996 transparency reforms sharply improved market quality.[^40]

A second limitation is definitional. "Post-mandatory" is analytically useful but historically imprecise because the old regime was dismantled in layers rather than in one clean repeal. The Order Handling Rules and Actual Size reforms changed the economics of dealer obligation in 1997; Regulation ATS further integrated ECNs in 1998; OATS changed the audit trail in 1998; CAT broadened it again after 2012; and FINRA did not eliminate the OATS rules until 2021. This report therefore treats the functional break as more important than the last surviving formal remnant of the old SOES structure.[^41]
