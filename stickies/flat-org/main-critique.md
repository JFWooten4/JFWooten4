> The question I wish someone would ask in one of these Valve interviews is, how do you make sure that the thankless chores that nobody wants to do actually get done? I get why Valve doesn’t have to sweat too much over their core functions of making and shipping great games, but how do you ensure that the trash gets emptied and the toilets get cleaned? Sure, you can contract those things out, but that doesn’t actually solve the problem because contracts have to be managed. I can tell you from painful experience that few things are more soul-crushing to a creative person (or less appreciated by their peers) than administering a contract. Do game designers at Valve really volunteer to take time out from developing games to bawl out vendors who screw up their invoicing?

> On a related topic, how do you ensure that the unsung heroes (the guys who you never notice unless something goes wrong) are appropriately rewarded? The best computer sysadmin I ever worked with looked to casual observers like he just sat around goofing off all day. In reality he had automated most routine tasks, and when something broke he sprang into action and got it fixed, usually before the users noticed it was down. Would a guy like that get good peer reviews at Valve?
