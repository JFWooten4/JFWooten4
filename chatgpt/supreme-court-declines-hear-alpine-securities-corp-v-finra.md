# Supreme Court Declines to Hear Alpine Securities Corp. v. FINRA

The U.S. Supreme Court building in Washington, D.C. On June 2, 2025, the U.S. Supreme Court denied the petition for a writ of certiorari in Alpine Securities Corporation v. Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA).[^1] This means the Supreme Court will not hear the Alpine v. FINRA case, leaving in place the prior ruling of the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals. In declining to take up Alpine’s appeal, the Supreme Court has effectively ended Alpine’s judicial challenge against FINRA’s enforcement action.[^1]

## Background of the Case

Alpine Securities Corporation, a broker-dealer based in Salt Lake City, faced an enforcement proceeding by FINRA – the self-regulatory organization overseeing broker-dealers.[^2] FINRA accused Alpine of misappropriating over $54.5 million from its customers by charging excessive fees and misusing client funds, and it sought to expel Alpine from FINRA membership as a sanction.[^2] In response, Alpine filed a lawsuit to block the expulsion, arguing that FINRA’s structure and authority to enforce rules were unconstitutional. Specifically, Alpine claimed that FINRA, as a private entity, was exercising regulatory power in violation of the "private nondelegation" doctrine – a principle that limits how federal agencies or Congress can delegate governmental powers to private organizations like FINRA.[^3][^4]

## D.C. Circuit Court Ruling

Alpine’s challenge proceeded in the lower courts before reaching the Supreme Court. In those proceedings, Alpine sought to stop FINRA’s expedited enforcement action while its constitutional claims were litigated. The U.S. Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit gave Alpine a partial victory on one issue: it ruled that FINRA cannot expel a member firm in an expedited proceeding without allowing review by the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC).[^5] The D.C. Circuit found that the lack of SEC oversight before a FINRA expulsion likely violated the private nondelegation doctrine, so FINRA must permit SEC appellate review of any expulsion decision.[^5] However, the appeals court declined to halt (enjoin) FINRA’s ongoing enforcement proceeding against Alpine and did not rule on Alpine’s broader constitutional arguments about FINRA’s overall structure.[^5] In other words, FINRA was allowed to continue its disciplinary process against Alpine (since any harm could be remedied by later SEC review if needed), and Alpine’s major constitutional claims remained unresolved at that stage.[^6]

## Supreme Court Petition and Denial

After the D.C. Circuit’s decision, Alpine Securities petitioned the U.S. Supreme Court to review the case and address its constitutional challenges to FINRA’s authority. The case was docketed at the Supreme Court as Alpine Securities Corp. v. FINRA, No. 24-904, in February 2025.[^7] Alpine’s petition urged the Court to consider whether FINRA’s enforcement powers – granted under federal law to a private self-regulatory body – violate constitutional principles, including Article II’s Executive Power and the nondelegation doctrine. Both FINRA and the U.S. Department of Justice (on behalf of the SEC and the federal government) opposed Alpine’s petition, with the government arguing that this was "an especially poor vehicle for review" of those issues.[^8]

On June 2, 2025, the Supreme Court denied Alpine’s petition for certiorari.[^1] In doing so, the Court declined to hear the case, meaning it refused to take up Alpine’s appeal. The denial was issued without any written opinion or noted dissents (as is common when the Supreme Court denies certiorari). News outlets reported that the justices "turned away" Alpine’s challenge, leaving intact the lower court’s ruling that allowed FINRA’s enforcement action to proceed.[^9][^10] The Supreme Court’s one-line order simply disposed of the petition, confirming that it would not review the constitutional questions Alpine raised.[^11]

## Implications of the Court’s Decision

Because the Supreme Court will not hear Alpine v. FINRA, the D.C. Circuit’s judgment stands as the final word on this case.[^1] The immediate effect is that FINRA can move forward with its enforcement action against Alpine Securities. FINRA’s authority to operate as a self-regulatory organization and to bring enforcement actions remains unchallenged by the Supreme Court’s decision.[^12] Alpine’s expulsion (or other sanctions imposed by FINRA) can take effect once the process is complete (including any SEC review of an expulsion decision), and Alpine now has no further judicial recourse on these claims.

More broadly, the Supreme Court’s refusal to grant certiorari leaves unresolved the larger constitutional questions about FINRA’s structure and the limits of delegating regulatory power to a private entity. Those issues could potentially be raised again in the future by other firms in similar disputes, but for now the high court chose not to weigh in.[^13] Notably, on the same day the petition was denied, FINRA itself took steps to fortify its processes against such challenges: on June 2, 2025, FINRA filed proposed rule changes to ensure that any expulsion or similar severe sanction would be subject to SEC review before becoming final.[^14] This rule change is aimed at addressing the concern identified by the D.C. Circuit – providing a safeguard that a government regulator (the SEC) oversees FINRA’s most serious disciplinary actions.[^14][^15]

In summary, the Supreme Court is not going to hear Alpine Securities Corp. v. FINRA. The petition for review was denied on June 2, 2025, leaving the D.C. Circuit’s decision in place and allowing FINRA’s enforcement proceedings (with SEC oversight for any expulsion) to continue uninterrupted.[^1][^9] This outcome means Alpine’s constitutional challenge has reached the end of the road, and FINRA’s regulatory authority remains intact under the existing legal framework.
