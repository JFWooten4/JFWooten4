# Executive Summary

Technical-writing courses typically start with writing fundamentals (audience analysis, clarity, ethics, research) and proceed to genre‐specific skills (e.g. memos, reports, proposals, instructions, visual design, and oral presentation). Most curricula emphasize audience/purpose, writing process (plan, draft, revise), style/mechanics, collaboration, and professional ethics【60†L93-L104】【51†L303-L312】. Learning outcomes focus on producing documents that meet workplace needs (clear, correct, ethical, audience‐appropriate) and using graphics and formatting effectively【60†L93-L104】【53†L548-L557】. Common assignments include short memos/emails, instruction manuals, technical descriptions, lab or progress reports, proposals, and oral/visual presentations. Assessment is usually project-based (often 60–80% of the grade), with rubrics evaluating content, organization, style, and graphics【25†L118-L126】【73†L301-L308】. Participation, peer review, quizzes/discussion tasks, and sometimes a final exam or portfolio make up the rest. Key texts include Markel & Selber’s Technical Communication【53†L536-L545】, Lannon & Gurak’s Technical Communication【62†L732-L741】, Alred et al.’s Handbook of Technical Writing【56†L830-L838】, and open texts (e.g. Technical Writing Essentials【32†L39-L43】). Discipline- or level-specific variations include a greater emphasis on lab reports/proposals and teamwork in engineering or science courses, and simpler genres and more writing-process instruction in introductory classes【58†L206-L214】【42†L358-L364】. Teaching methods center on hands-on practice: analyzing sample documents, collaborative projects, peer editing, writing workshops, and case studies. Rubrics typically break down projects by criteria such as accuracy, clarity, format, visuals, and mechanics【25†L118-L126】.

## Core Topics and Units

College technical-writing classes cover writing processes and principles (e.g. planning, drafting, revision, audience/purpose analysis, ethical/legal issues) and genre conventions. Typical units include: audience analysis and document purpose; research methods and information literacy; organization and style (conciseness, plain language, grammar); collaborative writing; and document design (use of headings, lists, graphics)【51†L303-L312】【53†L432-L441】. In Part 4 of Markel’s text, for example, students study specific workplace genres: email and letters; resumes/cover letters; technical definitions and descriptions; instructions/procedures; informal and formal reports; proposals; presentations; and web/social media content【62†L732-L741】. An open-course module similarly instructs students in "common types of reports, special format items such as lists and headings, [and] simple techniques for creating and using graphics in reports"【58†L206-L214】. In short, courses integrate (a) writing fundamentals (rhetoric, clarity, editing) and (b) workplace genres (memos/emails, data graphics, instructions, proposals, formal reports, etc.)【58†L206-L214】【51†L303-L312】.

## Typical Weekly/Module Sequence

Most semester plans begin with foundations (course overview, audience/purpose, style), then proceed to short document practice (e.g. memos, emails, definitions) before longer projects. Mid-semester units often cover procedures/manuals, data presentation/graphics, and intermediate reports. The latter part of the term is often devoted to major writing projects (e.g. a research report or proposal) and oral/visual communication. Finally, courses wrap up with presentations of final projects, peer review, and portfolio/reflection activities. For example, one engineering syllabus devotes Weeks 1–4 to introductory topics and memos, Weeks 5–8 to instructions and data charts, Weeks 9–11 to lab reports, and Weeks 12–14 to a team project presentation【27†L261-L270】. Another introductory course follows a similar arc (intro/grammar → memos & emails → user manuals & graphics → reports & proposals → final presentations).

## Essential Skills and Learning Outcomes

Courses emphasize audience-focused writing and clarity. Students learn to "recognize, analyze, and accommodate diverse audiences"【60†L93-L100】 and to "produce documents appropriate to audience, purpose, and genre"【60†L93-L100】. They practice ethical/professional style (avoiding bias and plagiarism)【60†L95-L100】 and learn to write concisely with correct grammar. Another central outcome is information literacy: students must "locate, evaluate, and incorporate pertinent information" into their documents【60†L97-L104】. Technical writing courses also train students to use visuals and design (charts, tables, illustrative figures) and often require creating slides or diagrams. Collaboration and peer review are stressed: for example, learning outcomes may include developing verbal, visual, and multimedia materials (individually and collaboratively)【60†L98-L104】 and participating in team communication. In sum, graduates should be able to plan and execute workplace documents (memos, reports, manuals, proposals) that are well-organized, concise, and usable by their intended readers【60†L93-L104】【51†L303-L312】.

## Common Assignments and Assessment Methods

Typical writing assignments include:

- Short workplace documents: Memos, emails, letters, and definitions. (Often practiced early in the semester.)

- Instructional materials: Procedure descriptions, how-to guides or user manuals with step-by-step instructions.

- Technical descriptions/specifications: Brief descriptions of products, processes, or systems.

- Reports: Informal and formal reports such as lab reports, progress reports, analytical or evaluation reports. For example, one course asks students to write a "Technical Description" and a field‐specific analytical report【73†L270-L278】【70†L260-L264】.

- Proposals: Persuasive documents such as project proposals or feasibility studies. Many courses include at least one small proposal.

- Presentations: Oral/visual projects (typically team-based) using slides or posters. Engineering courses frequently culminate in a group presentation of a technical project【22†L21-L27】.

- Job-search documents: Resumes and cover letters (especially in business-focused sections or chapters【53†L521-L530】).

- Other media: Some courses assign web writing, social media posts, or instructional videos.

Assessments rely heavily on project grades. Most instructors grade each major document with a detailed rubric (covering content, organization, style, visuals, mechanics)【25†L118-L126】. For instance, the Ohio State ECE syllabus notes that each assignment is "assessed in multiple categories" with an instructor‐provided rubric【25†L118-L126】. Typical grade breakdowns allocate 60–80% of the course grade to writing projects. For example, one syllabus weights three major papers at 15–35% each (total ~65%) and a final presentation at 15%【22†L21-L27】. The remainder often comes from quizzes, in‐class assignments, participation, or portfolios. Many classes include peer-review or writing workshops as graded activities, and informal tasks (homework exercises, mini-cases, weekly discussion posts) to reinforce concepts. Traditional exams are rare; one course included only a short final essay exam (50 points) as a small percentage of the grade【73†L301-L308】, while another explicitly has "no exams"【25†L118-L126】. Florida Poly’s syllabus, for example, shows 20% each for a portfolio, team project, report/presentation, and quizzes/discussions (totaling 95%)【65†L122-L127】, illustrating the project-centered approach.

## Industry-Relevant Document Types Taught

Courses deliberately cover the documents common in technical and business fields. Alongside memos and emails, instructors teach reports and proposals that mirror professional practice. For example, Markel’s table of contents includes chapters on "Workplace Memos and Letters," "Technical Descriptions, Specifications, and Marketing Materials," "Instructions and Procedures," "Informal Reports," "Formal Analytical Reports," and "Proposals"【62†L732-L741】. In practice, students write proposals, feasibility studies, and recommendation reports as taught genres. User guides and instruction sets (with figures or screenshots) are typical assignments. Graphic communication skills are practiced via charts, tables, and infographics. Some courses also include job-application documents: resumes, cover letters, and interview letters【53†L521-L530】. Overall, the curriculum integrates both written and visual/written hybrids that engineers or technical professionals encounter on the job【53†L521-L530】【62†L732-L741】. Alred et al.’s Handbook of Technical Writing underscores this breadth: "From abstracts to online professional profiles, from blogs and forums to formal reports and manuals," covering virtually every workplace genre【56†L830-L838】.

## Classroom Activities and Teaching Methods

Technical writing pedagogy is highly interactive. Common methods include:

- Peer Review and Workshops: Students critique each other’s drafts using guided rubrics. Many syllabi require mandatory peer feedback before submission.

- Collaborative Projects: Team assignments (e.g. group proposals or reports) mimic workplace collaboration. One course includes a "team charter" and collective tasks【42†L358-L364】.

- Hands-On Exercises: In-class editing of sample texts, grammar/style drills, or formatting exercises. For example, instructors often use "Document Analysis Activities" (from textbooks) where students evaluate model documents for learning【46†L1292-L1301】.

- Discussion and Reflection: Online or face-to-face discussions, journal entries, and reflection essays. For example, a fully online course grades weekly discussions and journal entries worth 40% of the grade, to "explore and practice methods, strategies, and principles of technical communication"【37†L123-L130】. Another syllabus lists weekly "engagement activities" (discussion board or wiki tasks) designed to apply content and simulate writing scenarios【35†L228-L236】.

- Case Studies: Real-world scenarios link writing tasks to professional contexts. One class, for instance, used a case study of a manmade disaster as the basis for a technical description assignment【70†L260-L264】.

- Presentations and Demonstrations: Students deliver tech reports or project pitches, often practicing slide creation and speaking skills.

- Technology Tools: Use of word processing, design, and collaboration software (e.g. Office 365, Google Docs, or LaTeX) is common. Courses often introduce citation managers and specialized templates.

## Rubrics and Evaluation Criteria

Most technical-writing assignments are scored with analytic rubrics that break down the grade by criterion. Typical rubric categories include accuracy of content, organization and structure, clarity/conciseness, correct style (tone, grammar, spelling), use of graphics, and proper formatting. In one example syllabus, "each category is weighted equally" on the rubric for every project【25†L118-L126】. Thus, a report might be evaluated on (say) 20% content accuracy, 20% document design, 20% language clarity, etc. Instructors also emphasize adherence to professional conventions (e.g. IMRaD sections in lab reports, memo format, APA/IEEE citation style). Many rubrics incorporate a usability or audience perspective (ease of navigation, helpful visuals). The Handbook of Technical Writing highlights this by listing checklists and sample documents for each genre【56†L830-L838】. Overall, evaluation is transparent and criterion-based, often shared with students ahead of each assignment.

Textbooks. Common texts include:

- Open Textbooks. Many instructors use free texts. For example, one syllabus uses Technical Writing Essentials by Last et al. (2019), an open-press book【32†L39-L43】. Additional open resources include Open Technical Communication and Ohio State’s Business and Professional Writing (both online)【37†L74-L82】.

## Variations by Discipline and Level

Discipline differences. Engineering and STEM-oriented courses tend to emphasize lab/experiment reports, data analysis, and teamwork. For example, the OSU ECE course includes a required lab report and data graphics assignment【22†L21-L27】, and the SFA course centers on engineering proposals and science-communication projects【42†L347-L356】. Business or communications programs may add more on resumes, business reports, and ethics case studies. Health-sciences writing classes (outside our examples) often include grant/protocol writing and patient education materials.

Course level differences. Introductory (100–200 level) courses cover a broad range of documents and spend more time on basic skills (sentence clarity, paragraph structure, audience analysis)【58†L206-L214】. Upper-division or specialized tech-writing courses may require a larger capstone project (e.g. a lengthy feasibility report or engineering design document) and may assume mastery of fundamentals. Advanced courses often stress teamwork and presentations (as in engineering capstone contexts) or discipline-specific genres (e.g. user manuals in computer science). A writing-in-the-disciplines example (ENG 381) explicitly assumes prior composition courses and focuses on "explaining difficult information to a non-expert audience" across a variety of media【67†L25-L33】【69†L79-L81】.

## Rubrics and Evaluation Criteria

Instructors commonly use clear rubrics for major projects. These usually allocate points or weights for categories such as Content/Accuracy, Organization, Clarity/Style, Technical Format (headings, captions), Graphics/Visuals, and Mechanics (grammar/spelling). For instance, one syllabus notes that "rubrics across assignments will remain similar" and that each submission "will be assessed in multiple categories"【25†L118-L126】. Many rubrics are "audience-centered": they check whether the writer has met the needs of the expected reader. In addition, courses often require correct citation of sources (APA, IEEE, etc.) as part of the format grade. Complete meeting of assignment requirements (length, format, due date) is also factored into the score. These criteria are typically shared with students in assignment sheets.

## Recommended Readings and Resources

Textbook resources include both proprietary and open-access materials. Beyond the texts noted above, instructors often recommend authoritative style guides and references. For example, one syllabus expects students to consult APA, IEEE, or CSE handbooks for citation format【60†L93-L100】. The Handbook of Technical Writing【56†L830-L838】 is frequently recommended as a reference. Many courses provide students with PDF handouts or link to free online manuals (e.g. the Purdue OWL’s technical writing pages).

Additional materials:

- Case and sample documents (often from sources like the Document-Based Cases for Technical Communication collection). Textbooks like Markel’s include Document Analysis Activities to apply concepts【46†L1292-L1301】.

- Writing tools: Guidance on using software (e.g. Word "Track Changes" or LaTeX) may be offered.

- Professional guidelines: Some instructors cover technical communication standards in specific fields (e.g. IEEE-Eta Kappa Nu reports, or OSHA guidelines for safety writing).

- Journal articles or handbooks: Advanced classes might read articles on topics like readability or ethics.

- Career resources: Guidance on resumes and cover letters is often drawn from career-services materials.

Open educational resources are increasingly used. For example, Last et al.’s Technical Writing Essentials (2019) is an open-source course text used at Texas A&M Texarkana【32†L39-L43】. Andrews (TAMU–Corpus Christi) directs students to free online texts (Open TC, Pressbooks modules)【37†L74-L82】. These ensure affordability and reflect current digital communication practices.

Industry links: Instructors may also share websites for technical communicators (e.g. Society for Technical Communication) or professional blogs (like the Google Developers’ technical writing series). Membership in relevant associations and reading in journals such as IEEE Transactions on Professional Communication or Technical Communication Quarterly may be encouraged for career-minded students.
