Extensive searches of Fox News and related sites found no Fox News article explicitly using the term "insolvency without reconciliation." The only relevant Fox News pieces were opinion columns (by Justin Haskins and Jack McPherrin) discussing UCC Article 8 and retirement accounts.[^1][^2] These do not contain the phrase "without reconciliation" and do not refer to any specific Florida case. We found no other news outlet (national or Florida media) reporting such a story, aside from one content-farm summary that paraphrases the Fox opinion. Florida law (UCC Article 8, Fla. Ch. 678) already addresses broker insolvency. It does not use the term "reconciliation," but instead provides that once bankruptcy proceedings commence, customers’ pro rata claims attach to all remaining securities.[^3] Importantly, Florida Stat. §678.5041 prohibits pledging customer assets without consent[^4] and §678.5031(4) allows recovery of wrongfully transferred assets.[^5] Thus Fox’s claim that secured creditors can seize client assets "even if improperly pledged" is misleading. In fact, Florida statutes give customers priority (subject to narrow exceptions[^6]) and allow trustees to claw back improper transfers. No Florida cases or PACER filings were found on this issue. In short, the Fox piece oversimplifies and dramatizes UCC Article 8: it raises valid concerns about intermediary insolvency risks, but ignores existing statutory protections.

Florida Law and Court Records: Florida’s statutes adopt Revised UCC Article 8 (enacted 1998–1999). Key provisions: - Fla. Stat. §678.5011–678.5031 define "security entitlement" and a customer’s ownership interest. Customers hold security entitlements (claims), not certificate title.[^12] Statutory text: "To the extent necessary…all interests in that financial asset held by the securities intermediary are…held…for the entitlement holders, [and] are not subject to claims of creditors of the securities intermediary".[^5] - Insolvency rules: Fla. Stat. §678.5031(4) provides that, if insolvency proceedings are initiated and the intermediary lacks enough securities, then a trustee can recover missing assets from third-party purchasers.[^5] Subsections (a–d) enumerate the conditions (insolvency, shortfall, violation of duties) under which entitlement holders may enforce claims. This matches the UCC scheme: in bankruptcy, investors assert pro rata rights to the remaining pool of securities.[^5] - Priority: Fla. Stat. §678.5111 governs priority. Generally, entitlement holders outrank secured creditors if a broker falls short.[^6] Only a secured creditor who had "control" of the asset (like under Article 9) can leapfrog customers.[^13] This means an investor’s claim is typically senior unless the broker explicitly gave "control" to the lender (e.g., by dedicating assets as collateral in an Article 9 manner).

We found no Florida court cases or filings specifically on these UCC Article 8 issues. Search of PACER/state court databases for keywords (e.g. "678.5031", "securities intermediary insolvency", "Florida UCC Article 8") turned up nothing relevant. No litigation is reported where Florida’s statutes were interpreted in this context, at least through early 2026.

"Insolvency without Reconciliation" Explained: This is not a legal term in Florida law; rather, it describes a scenario. It refers to a broker-dealer becoming insolvent before it has balanced (reconciled) all customer accounts for the day’s trades. In that case, as shown by a Uniform Law Comm’n example, the firm might owe more shares of XYZ than it holds, and less of ABC.[^9] Under UCC Article 8, the result is that customers’ entitlement shares are apportioned among the actual holdings (short or long). Florida law achieves this via §678.5031(4): once insolvency is filed and a shortage exists, all entitlement holders share pro rata in the remaining securities.[^5] The firm’s obligation to "maintain" sufficient assets (Fla. Stat. §678.5041) also plays a role: it forbids pledging customer securities without agreement,[^4] so any "improper" transfer can be unwound under §678.5031(4). Thus, "insolvency without reconciliation" essentially means the insolvency exception in Article 8 has been triggered, not a separate rule. Florida’s statutes embody this: they do not impose a formal "reconciliation" step, but ensure fairness through pro rata distribution and trustee recovery rights.[^5][^6]

Accuracy of Reporting vs. Law: The Fox News opinion pieces raise genuine issues about Article 8’s structure, but they are informal and selective. For example, Fox states: "Article 8 permits secured creditors to seize customer assets… even if the securities were improperly pledged".[^7] Legally, Florida §678.5041(2) forbids pledging maintained customer assets without consent.[^4] If an intermediary does so, §678.5031(4) allows recovery: the trustee can claw back those assets or have the purchaser replace them.[^5] In other words, secured creditors may temporarily take customer-collateralized assets, but only until a court sorts out priority; an improper pledge is not a free "gravy train." Fox’s phrasing ("even if improperly pledged") overlooks these safeguards. Similarly, Fox implies investors lose all direct ownership, which is true in technical form (they hold entitlements),[^5] but it also omits that state law explicitly protects those entitlements from most creditors.[^6] In sum, the news claims are partially correct but overstated. The primary legal records (Florida’s UCC §§678.5031–678.5041 and 678.5111) show a more nuanced picture: customers generally have priority and remedies that the Fox articles ignore.[^5][^6]
