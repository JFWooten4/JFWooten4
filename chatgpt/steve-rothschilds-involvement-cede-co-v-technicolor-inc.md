# Steve Rothschild’s Involvement in Cede & Co. v. Technicolor, Inc.

## Case Background

The dispute Cede & Co. v. Technicolor, Inc. arose from the 1982–83 takeover of Technicolor by investor Ronald Perelman’s MacAndrews & Forbes (MAF). In a two-step cash-out merger completed in January 1983, MAF’s subsidiary was merged into Technicolor, and minority shareholders were bought out.[^1][^2] A minority shareholder (Cinerama, Inc., with Cede & Co. as record holder) dissented and pursued a Delaware statutory appraisal action to contest the fair value of their Technicolor shares.[^3] Later, upon discovering evidence of alleged wrongdoing in the merger, the shareholder also filed a fiduciary duty lawsuit (a "fraud" or personal liability action) against Technicolor’s board and Perelman’s affiliates for unfair dealing.[^4] These twin proceedings – an appraisal for fair value and a fraud claim for damages – were litigated concurrently through a lengthy trial and multiple Delaware Supreme Court appeals over more than a decade.[^5][^6]

## Steve Rothschild’s Role and Involvement

Legal Counsel for Technicolor: Steve Rothschild was not a party to the case, but rather one of the lead attorneys representing Technicolor. At the time of the merger litigation, Rothschild was a partner at Skadden, Arps, Slate, Meagher & Flom, the law firm retained to defend Technicolor in the appraisal proceeding.[^7] In late February 1983, shortly after the merger, Rothschild brought in a junior colleague (Thomas Allingham II) to assist – essentially assigning Allingham to handle the day-to-day case as a new associate, while Rothschild oversaw the matter as the senior attorney.[^8] Allingham later recounted that Rothschild "had no interest whatsoever in technical corporate valuation issues" and was happy to let him take the lead on developing the case, with Rothschild supervising in the early years.[^9] In other words, Rothschild’s involvement was primarily as outside counsel managing Technicolor’s legal defense, not as an executive or decision-maker within the company.

Timeline of Rothschild’s Participation: Rothschild’s contributions unfolded during the initial phases of the litigation and early settlement talks, and then receded as the case progressed to trial:

- Early 1983: Rothschild and his firm took on Technicolor’s representation in the appraisal lawsuit immediately after the merger. He assigned an associate to build the defense and conduct discovery, while he remained in a supervisory role.[^8][^9]

- Spring 1983: Rothschild engaged in settlement negotiations with the shareholder’s counsel (Gary Greenberg). The two sides came within roughly $200,000 of an agreement (at about $31–$32 per share), a narrow gap of only a few percent.[^10] However, the tentative deal fell through when Rothschild reported he "couldn’t sell it" – i.e. he could not get his client’s final approval – so the case did not settle at that time.[^11] This early attempt shows Rothschild’s influence in trying to resolve the dispute, though ultimately no settlement was reached.

- 1986: Approximately three years into the appraisal litigation, Cinerama filed a separate fraud/unfair-dealing suit after discovering new evidence. This greatly expanded the scope of the case – now Technicolor’s directors, MAF, and Ronald Perelman were added as defendants in the fraud action.[^12] With the new claims, additional legal teams joined the defense (e.g. Richards, Layton & Finger was hired to represent Technicolor’s individual directors).[^7] Rothschild continued to be involved, but the litigation now had multiple attorneys coordinating defenses for different parties.

- Late 1980s (Pre-Trial): As the case headed toward a massive combined trial (the appraisal and fraud actions were tried together in 1989–1990), the makeup of Technicolor’s legal team evolved. Thomas Allingham – by then a relatively young partner – had effectively become the hands-on lead for Technicolor’s case. On the eve of trial, a senior Delaware litigator, Rodman Ward Jr., was brought in to provide overarching supervision.[^13] This shift suggests that Rothschild’s day-to-day role had diminished by the trial stage, with other attorneys taking primary responsibility for courtroom presentation. Even so, Rothschild was still part of the defense team through this period; contemporaneous accounts indicate he remained involved behind the scenes and in supporting the trial work.

Not a Witness or Executive: Importantly, Steve Rothschild did not serve as a witness in the proceedings, nor did he have any executive or shareholder role at Technicolor. His name does not appear among the parties or testifying witnesses in the court opinions – all the defendants were Technicolor itself, its board members at the time of the merger, and Perelman (and his companies) as the acquirer.[^14] Rothschild’s capacity was strictly that of outside legal counsel. He participated by advising his client, handling negotiations, and litigating (examining witnesses, briefing, argument), but he never gave sworn testimony in the case. In other words, he was an advocate, not a fact witness, and he had no personal stake in Technicolor’s stock or corporate decisions.

## Influence on the Case and Outcome

Legal Impact vs. Counsel’s Role: The outcome of Cede & Co. v. Technicolor ultimately turned on legal principles of Delaware corporate law and the factual record of the merger, rather than on any individual lawyer’s personal influence. As counsel for Technicolor, Rothschild certainly affected the defense strategy – for example, his team’s efforts led the Court of Chancery at trial to appraise Technicolor’s shares at only $21.60 per share (as of the merger date), which was below the $23 merger price.[^2][^15] This low valuation meant the Chancery court found no damages and entered judgment in favor of Technicolor and its directors on the fraud claims.[^16] In that sense, the defense (to which Rothschild contributed early on) was successful at the trial stage. However, the case did not end there – the Delaware Supreme Court intervened multiple times, and its rulings were driven by doctrinal issues, not by any behind-the-scenes dealings by counsel.

On appeal, the Delaware Supreme Court issued several landmark decisions in the Technicolor saga. For instance, in Technicolor I (1988) the high court held that Cinerama could pursue both the appraisal and the fraud action concurrently, overturning a Chancery ruling that would have forced an election of remedies.[^17] In later appeals, the Supreme Court clarified how the fair value should be determined – notably Technicolor II (1993) and a 1996 decision found that the Chancery had erred by excluding MAF’s post-merger plans for Technicolor from the valuation, and ordered a re-appraisal that considered those plans.[^18] These appellate decisions hinged on interpreting Delaware’s appraisal statute and fiduciary duty law (applying precedents like Weinberger v. UOP), and they corrected legal errors by the trial court.[^18][^19] There is no indication in the court opinions that any argument or action by Rothschild uniquely shaped these outcomes – the Supreme Court’s focus was on the law and the evidence, not on the conduct of individual attorneys.

Outcome and Rothschild’s Impact: In the end, Rothschild’s participation did not independently alter the final result of the litigation, beyond the ordinary role of competent counsel. The protracted case was ultimately resolved by judicial determinations of fair value and fairness, with the courts making their decisions based on financial analyses and fiduciary obligations. If anything, Rothschild’s most tangible "influence" could have been his attempt to settle the case early in 1983 – had that ~$32/share settlement succeeded, the famous Technicolor litigation (and its numerous court rulings) might have been avoided.[^10][^11] But since the deal fell apart, the case proceeded to be decided on its merits. The final stages saw Technicolor obliged to pay a recalculated fair value for the shares (eventually around $28.41 per share after further proceedings in the late 1990s),[^20] a result determined by expert valuations under court supervision, not by any negotiation or testimony from Rothschild. In summary, Steve Rothschild served as a key legal advocate for Technicolor in the early-to-mid phases of Cede & Co. v. Technicolor, but he had no official role as a witness or corporate actor. His involvement provided skilled representation for Technicolor, yet the decisive factors in the litigation were the legal standards applied and the factual evidence of Technicolor’s value and merger process – factors ultimately weighed by the Delaware courts.[^18][^19]
