# Why DAWSON Could Cost Around $1.5 Million Per Year While PACER and CM/ECF Modernization Is Estimated Near $125 Million Per Year

## Executive summary

The large apparent cost gap between the U.S. Tax Court's DAWSON system and the federal judiciary's PACER and CM/ECF modernization is real, but much of it is explained by scope, scale, and what is actually being counted rather than by a simple difference in software engineering efficiency. The Tax Court reported that from project initiation in FY 2018 through launch in FY 2021, DAWSON cost about $5.6 million total, including the Flexion agile development contract, 18F support, and security testing. If that total is spread across four fiscal years, it comes out to roughly $1.4 million per year, which is the rough origin of the "about $1.5 million/year" claim. But the Tax Court's own later budget documents also show that DAWSON is not costless to sustain: in late FY 2024 the Court obligated $3.1 million for DAWSON, split between $1.95 million of operations and maintenance and $1.15 million of continued software development/modernization, and then entered a five-year IT contract supporting DAWSON through FY 2030.[^1]

By contrast, the judiciary's PACER and CM/ECF modernization estimate is not for a single court or for an MVP-style initial build. The judiciary now says modernization will cost $700 million to $800 million over six years ending in FY 2030, which implies an average development spend of roughly $117 million to $133 million per year, i.e. "on the order of $125 million/year." In FY 2026 alone, the judiciary's Electronic Public Access program shows $53.1 million for modernization and $177.9 million for maintaining current critical public access services, for total EPA program requirements of $231.0 million. This means the public discussion often compares DAWSON's early build cost against a federal-judiciary-wide modernization program that includes parallel operations, security hardening, public access infrastructure, and migration risk management. That is a major apples-to-oranges problem.[^2]

There is also a profound difference in operational scope. DAWSON supports a single national Article I court with about 18,549 cases filed in FY 2025, around 250 internal users cited in the Tax Court's 2018 RFQ, and approximately 25,000 registered Tax Court practitioners. PACER and CM/ECF support more than 200 federal courts, more than 5.3 million PACER user accounts, over 621 million requests for case information in FY 2025, and access to more than 1 billion filed documents. The federal judiciary handled roughly 41,824 appellate filings, 382,692 district court filings, and 557,376 bankruptcy filings in 2025. These are not comparable service footprints.[^3]

Technically, DAWSON was procured as a relatively contained agile rebuild. Its RFQ used GSA Schedule 70, capped the task order at $2 million per year for one base year plus two option years, required free/open-source software, disallowed license and subscription fees in contractor quotes, specified FedRAMP-certified cloud hosting controlled by the Court, required APIs between major components, and explicitly said the contractor would not be responsible for importing pending case data or practitioner data. The Court itself kept responsibility for hosting and the Authority to Operate.[^4]

The judiciary's PACER and CM/ECF situation is much harder. 18F's 2021 path analysis found outdated foundational technology, decentralization and customization that created instability and high maintenance cost, a backlog of more than 15,000 requirements and bug fixes, legacy Perl code in the "Outserve" component composed of more than 1,100 scripts, an aging Informix database, and more than one billion rows of data in CM/ECF. 18F also found that the judiciary's decentralization and configurability, while tied to court autonomy, had produced thousands of customization options with resulting security and maintenance burdens. The modernization therefore has to replace not only application code, but also a decades-old distributed operating model while preserving court operations, sealed-document protections, and public access.[^5]

The strongest bottom-line conclusion is this: the cost differential is mostly explained by scale, heterogeneity, migration complexity, and the inclusion of parallel-run/security/public-access costs in the PACER number, not by a straightforward claim that the judiciary is spending eighty times more money for "the same thing." Even so, the official sources also support a serious critique: the judiciary could likely reduce costs by standardizing workflows more aggressively, limiting local customizations, reusing open-source components such as DAWSON where feasible, using more shared cloud and identity services, and maintaining smaller empowered product teams with phased delivery instead of large, diffuse requirements-driven programs. 18F explicitly recommended that the judiciary consider leveraging the Tax Court's open-source system and developing the new platform as a modern, loosely coupled, agile system rather than another monolithic build. The AO also reported it was exploring a hybrid COTS-plus-custom approach for parts of the solution, especially unified search.[^6]

## What the official numbers actually show

The cleanest official DAWSON number is the Tax Court's FY 2022 budget justification. It states that from project initiation in FY 2018 through launch in FY 2021, expenditures for the new electronic filing and case management system totaled approximately $5.6 million, including the Flexion award under GSA Schedule 70, 18F project management and technical assistance, and security vulnerability testing. The same document says DAWSON launched on December 28, 2020 and provided base functionality for petition filing and case management.[^7]

That means the popular "about $1.5 million/year" figure is not an official annual operating number. It is a rough annualization of the official build total. If one spreads $5.6 million over FY 2018, FY 2019, FY 2020, and FY 2021, the result is about $1.4 million/year; if spread over a shorter three-year build window, it is closer to $1.9 million/year. So the claim is directionally plausible but depends on the denominator chosen. That should be stated explicitly whenever the figure is used.[^7]

The Tax Court's later budget documents show that post-launch sustainment is material. In the FY 2026 budget justification, the Court says that in late FY 2024 it obligated $3.1 million for DAWSON, with $1.95 million counted as DAWSON operations and maintenance and $1.15 million counted as DAWSON software development/modernization. The same document says the Court signed a five-year IT contract in FY 2025 to provide DAWSON-related operations and maintenance through FY 2030, and it describes DAWSON as an open-source, cloud-based application.[^8]

The PACER modernization figure comes from a different type of estimate. In June 2026, the judiciary announced that modernization costs are estimated at $700 million to $800 million over a six-year period ending in FY 2030, with a substantial portion concentrated in FY 2026-FY 2028 because the project was accelerated for cybersecurity reasons. That is the source for the frequently quoted "about $125 million/year" number.[^9]

The judiciary's annual budget appendices show what sits around that six-year estimate. For FY 2025, EPA program requirements were projected at $187.2 million, including $44.4 million for modernization and $142.8 million to maintain current public-access services. For FY 2026, those requirements rose to $231.0 million, including $53.1 million for modernization and $177.9 million to maintain existing critical services. The FY 2026 appendix also notes that once the new system is operational, roughly $55.1 million in current annual NextGen CM/ECF requirements would be replaced by the new system's operations and maintenance and other contemporary EPA requirements.[^10]

So there are really three PACER-related numbers in play, and they are often conflated. The first is the six-year modernization development estimate of $700-$800 million. The second is the annual modernization appropriation line, such as $53.1 million in FY 2026. The third is the total annual EPA program cost, which includes legacy operations, communications infrastructure, public access services, and related security, totaling $231.0 million in FY 2026. Comparing any one of those numbers to DAWSON tells a somewhat different story.[^11]

## Scope, user base, and functional requirements

The U.S. Tax Court is a specialized national court with one core mission: adjudicating disputes between taxpayers and the IRS before payment of the disputed tax. Its 2018 RFQ describes a court with approximately 22,000 open cases, about 27,000 cases filed in FY 2017, around 250 internal users, and approximately 25,000 registered practitioners. Later budget documents show 18,549 cases filed in FY 2025, with about 78 percent of those cases filed by self-represented taxpayers, and the Court's trial operations spread across 74 designated trial cities. These characteristics matter because they produce a demanding but still relatively bounded workflow universe.[^22]

DAWSON's functional requirements were ambitious but focused. The RFQ's initial phases centered on identity and access management, case initiation, fee payment through Pay.gov, search, e-signatures, notes, and content management. The initial MVP was supposed to let external users file documents electronically, including starting a case, while internal court users routed those filings, acted on them, and served filings and orders. User stories covered pro se petition filing, paper and electronic workflows, sealed submissions, fee payments, search, public access, scanning of paper filings, template orders, individualized orders, activity logging, and later additions such as notifications, calendaring, analytics, and reporting.[^23]

The Tax Court's public-access model is also narrower and structurally cheaper. DAWSON is free to users. Parties get free access to their own cases, and non-parties get free access to docket records, orders, and opinions in unsealed cases. The public training guide shows that publicly viewable electronic documents are not "everything in every case"; they include opinions and orders, plus certain later-filed document classes such as post-trial briefs, amicus briefs, and stipulated decisions, while sealed cases or sealed documents are not viewable except by the parties.[^19]

PACER and CM/ECF have a much broader product surface. PACER is the judiciary-wide public portal for court dockets, case reports, and more than 1 billion documents filed in more than 200 federal courts. In FY 2025 alone, PACER processed over 621 million requests and had more than 5.3 million user accounts. CM/ECF is not just a public-access front end; it is the filing and case-management engine for the appellate, district, and bankruptcy courts, accessed by judges, staff, attorneys, trustees, bankruptcy claimants, and some pro se filers. That means the modernization must handle civil, criminal, bankruptcy, appellate, sealed, highly sensitive, and cross-court transfer workflows, not just one specialized docketing regime.[^24]

The judiciary's own documents show how much functionality is implicated. The FY 2027 EPA appendix says the new system must modernize both case management and PACER, and the June 2026 announcement says the Administrative Office has done extensive work mapping the processes for filing and managing all case types across district, appellate, and bankruptcy courts. The Long Range Plan adds that the new system is meant to improve communication, collaboration, and engagement among courts, the public, and other partners, with unified search as a key feature.[^25]

This scope difference alone explains much of the gap. DAWSON had to support one national court with a high share of self-represented litigants and a relatively unified body of rules. PACER and CM/ECF must support thousands of judges and staff, millions of accounts, many court types, and a public-access service used at national scale. It is not just "more users"; it is more user types, more case types, more compliance rules, more search traffic, more documents, and more organizational stakeholders.[^26]

## Technical architecture and migration burden

DAWSON was specified from the beginning as a modern rebuild. The Tax Court's RFQ required a web-based, mobile-friendly system hosted on a FedRAMP-certified cloud solution controlled by the Court. It required APIs to intermediate major components, no single point of failure, elastic compute, open-source encryption protocols, integration with Pay.gov, analytics and monitoring, continuous integration tooling, and an extensible software architecture. The Court also required high standards for test coverage, accessibility, and security, including 90 percent code coverage, WCAG 2.1 AA, and OWASP ASVS-based security testing.[^27]

Just as important, the Tax Court deliberately simplified some of the hardest modernization problems. The contractor was not responsible for importing pending case data, practitioner data, or other data into the new system. The Court retained responsibility for staging and production hosting, for obtaining the Authority to Operate, and for deciding and validating security controls. The RFQ also acknowledged that the legacy vendor license prevented disclosure of current-system source and designs, so the new system would not be built incrementally on top of the old one and would not run in tandem with it for a long overlap period.[^20]

PACER and CM/ECF face the opposite situation. 18F's path analysis found that the current judiciary platform had outdated foundational technology and unsustainable complexity. The report describes "Outserve," a central component, as legacy Perl code with inadequate automated testing and more than 1,100 different Perl scripts. It also found an aging Informix database, poor performance caused by over-normalization, a backlog of more than 15,000 requirements and bug fixes, and more than one billion rows of data in CM/ECF. At the same time, the existing system's decentralization and configurability had led to thousands of customization options, fueling unpredictable behavior, security problems, and high cost of change.[^21]

18F therefore recommended a new open-source platform built with modern technology and architecture, APIs, cloud options, and a loosely coupled delivery model rather than a single new monolith. The report also recommended moving off Informix to modern database and storage solutions and making cloud computing part of the replacement strategy. In other words, PACER modernization is not merely a front-end redesign or a lift-and-shift hosting move; it is a deep platform and data transformation program.[^28]

The judiciary's more recent planning documents confirm this. The FY 2026 Long Range Plan says NextGen CM/ECF is now in "lights on" mode, with fixes limited largely to vulnerabilities and required legal changes, while the Case Management Modernization effort replaces it. The FY 2027 JITF chapter says the judiciary's strategy is incremental rollout, with first delivery to pilot courts in January 2026, and emphasizes that the system will be cloud-based, implemented with modern data standards, a data catalog, and a data-governance framework.[^29]

The architectural comparison therefore cuts heavily in DAWSON's favor from a cost perspective. DAWSON started with a greenfield-like cloud/open-source/API posture, limited migration responsibility, contained workflows, and a single-court governance structure. PACER modernization is trying to replace a national judicial operating system while still running the old one, hardening security against attacks aimed at sealed documents, and preserving continuity for courts that depend on CM/ECF every day.[^30]

## Procurement, staffing, governance, and legal constraints

DAWSON was procured in a comparatively modern and disciplined way. The 2018 RFQ used GSA Schedule 70, SIN 132-51, sought agile software development services, and capped the order at $2 million for the first year plus $2 million for each of two option years. It was a time-and-materials task order; the Court said it would not consider quotes that included license or subscription fees, required all developed code to be delivered daily into a court-designated GitHub repository, and intended that delivered software and documentation would be owned by the Court and committed to the public domain. Key personnel were limited to a project manager and technical lead, and even contractor team members had to be pre-approved and supply a recent FBI Identity History Summary Check or equivalent clearance acceptable to the Court.[^27]

The delivery model was also intentionally small. The RFQ stated that the Court's internal management and staffing were not adequate to coordinate more than one development team initially. The Court designated an empowered Product Owner, used two-week sprint cycles, and said it might add a second contractor team only if development went smoothly. That is consistent with 18F's later guidance to the judiciary that an initial product team should be roughly 5 to 7 people, including a product owner, technical lead, UX roles, 2-3 developers, and part-time testing and security leads.[^31]

The judiciary-wide modernization has unfolded much more slowly and with many more institutional steps. An independent reassessment began in 2019; 18F's path analysis arrived in 2021; the AO launched the Case Management Modernization effort in March 2022; the AO simultaneously pursued market research through NCSC and an RFI for unified search; first functional software components went to pilot courts in January 2026; and in March 2026 the Judicial Conference's IT Committee endorsed accelerating as much work as possible into 2026 and 2027. That timeline reflects not just software delivery, but governance, market research, acquisition strategy, and branch-wide change management.[^32]

On governance and legal constraints, PACER and CM/ECF are also subject to a more complex access regime than many executive-branch systems. PACER is funded through judiciary user fees deposited into the JITF rather than ordinary FOIA-style disclosure rules, and the judiciary's privacy/access regime is driven by court rules, Judicial Conference policy, and statutes such as the E-Government Act rather than FOIA. Official federal guidance states that FOIA does not apply to records of the federal courts or judicial branch agencies. Instead, the judiciary has detailed rules for redaction of personal identifiers, sealed documents, criminal-file exclusions from public access, and transcript redaction.[^33]

Those privacy and access constraints are material cost drivers. The Judicial Conference policy requires redaction of Social Security numbers, minor names, financial account numbers, dates of birth, and in criminal cases home addresses; it excludes a range of criminal-case materials from public access and requires transcript review/redaction before remote availability. Court-level privacy pages likewise emphasize that PACER protects sealed documents and certain sensitive filing categories from public internet access. The 2026 modernization documents also say the judiciary has been a frequent target of sophisticated hackers seeking unauthorized access to sealed documents, which has accelerated both timeline and cost.[^34]

CJIS is a more limited factor in the PACER comparison than it may appear from public debate. The FBI's CJIS Security Policy applies to access to criminal justice information and to entities operating in support of those services. In the materials reviewed for PACER and CM/ECF, the dominant compliance themes are Judicial Conference privacy rules, sealing/redaction rules, FedRAMP/cloud controls, account security, and legacy-system hardening, not CJIS-specific requirements. Some judiciary systems outside PACER and CM/ECF do interact with CJIS, but the PACER modernization documents reviewed here do not identify CJIS as a primary governing framework for the modernization effort. That suggests CJIS may matter at the edges of some federal-court security ecosystems, but it does not seem to be the main explanation for the PACER cost differential.[^35]

## Why the cost differential is so large

The first and most important reason is that the comparison usually mixes DAWSON's initial build with PACER's branch-wide replacement plus parallel operations. DAWSON's official $5.6 million figure is a completed pre-launch build cost. The judiciary's FY 2026 budget, by contrast, shows that while it is spending $53.1 million to modernize CM/ECF and PACER, it is simultaneously spending $177.9 million to keep current public-access services operating. That alone can make the PACER program look far more expensive than a like-for-like "greenfield initial build" comparison would suggest.[^36]

The second reason is scale. Tax Court volume is meaningful but bounded: 18,549 filings in FY 2025, about 250 internal users in the RFQ-era description, around 25,000 registered practitioners, and a more limited public-document set. PACER supports more than 5.3 million accounts, 621 million annual requests, over 1 billion documents, and the combined filing volumes of appellate, district, and bankruptcy courts. Even if software costs do not scale linearly with users, infrastructure, indexing, support, search, identity, logging, monitoring, and abuse prevention all rise sharply at those levels.[^37]

The third reason is heterogeneity. The Tax Court is one court with one national rule set and one primary subject area. CM/ECF spans appellate, district, and bankruptcy practice, each with distinct workflows and legal constraints. The 2026 announcement states that the AO has mapped processes across all those court types to understand how the new system must function, and 18F found that the old system's heavy customization and decentralization were major drivers of maintenance cost and security risk. In other words, PACER modernization is paying not only for scale, but also for the cost of reconciling institutional diversity.[^38]

The fourth reason is migration risk. The Tax Court spared its contractor the hardest data-conversion responsibility and was able to build a new system without exposing the legacy codebase. The federal judiciary must replace legacy Perl and Informix components, rationalize decades of configuration drift, preserve access to a giant document corpus, and roll out in waves without disrupting the functioning of courts that depend on CM/ECF for day-to-day operations. 18F's warning that current technology is outdated and unsustainable, together with the judiciary's decision to run NextGen in "lights on" mode while building the successor, implies significant parallel-run cost.[^39]

The fifth reason is cybersecurity and sensitive-access risk. PACER and CM/ECF are not ordinary public websites. They contain sealed and highly sensitive materials, and the judiciary says the systems have been the target of sophisticated attempts to gain unauthorized access to sealed documents. To mitigate that, the modernization program is tied to secure cloud storage, stronger search/data architectures, and broader judiciary cybersecurity and IT-modernization efforts. The FY 2027 JITF chapter shows total court cybersecurity funding of approximately $148.8 million when EPA-funded cybersecurity activities are included, underscoring that CM/ECF modernization sits inside a much larger security envelope than DAWSON's original build budget.[^40]

The sixth reason is organizational overhead. The Tax Court's small-team, product-owner-led, one-court model naturally reduces coordination burden. The judiciary-wide effort has to move through AO leadership, the Judicial Conference, pathfinder courts, user groups, budget appendices, market research, RFI and procurement decisions, and a larger contractor and governance footprint. GAO's 2022 report criticizing judiciary IT management and calling for establishment of a CIO also suggests that some portion of today's higher cost reflects the judiciary correcting governance and enterprise-management weaknesses while modernizing the product itself.[^41]

## Plausible lower-cost alternatives and efficiencies

The official sources strongly support the view that the judiciary has room to lower cost, even if it cannot get remotely close to DAWSON's absolute spend. The clearest candidate is reuse. 18F explicitly recommended that the judiciary consider leveraging the Tax Court's open-source case-management system, noting that it already included filing a petition, docketing, quality control, workflow, notes, calendaring, sealed cases, and public searching, and that reuse of existing free codebases could reduce development time and cost. That does not mean DAWSON could be dropped into the federal judiciary unchanged. It does mean the judiciary itself had a credible official source telling it not to reinvent everything from scratch.[^49]

A second efficiency is standardization before customization. 18F found that thousands of customization options in CM/ECF were driving unpredictability, maintenance burden, and security problems. The recommendation for a single base SQL schema, APIs, and a loosely coupled product architecture points toward a model in which courts consume a more standardized national product with narrower local extension points. Cost savings here would come less from "writing less code once" and more from reducing the lifetime burden of testing, securing, documenting, and supporting thousands of variants.[^50]

A third efficiency is smaller empowered product teams and earlier incremental delivery. 18F's "How to get started building a new CM/ECF. Today." paper recommends beginning with one small cross-functional team of roughly 5-7 people, working on a single valuable slice of functionality, and warns against top-down requirements processes that delay learning. The Tax Court's own RFQ took a similar view by saying it could not effectively coordinate more than one development team at the start. These sources suggest that lower cost is tied not only to technology choices but to organizational discipline: narrow the first slice, validate it with users, and only then scale.[^51]

A fourth efficiency is hybrid procurement instead of total custom build. The AO reported in its FY 2023 EPA appendix that after agreeing with 18F that the existing system should be replaced, it engaged the National Center for State Courts to assess the market and concluded that while no single commercial product likely met all needs, a hybrid of COTS and custom development might accelerate delivery. The same appendix shows the AO pursuing a commercial-style unified-search capability through an RFI. That supports a lower-cost architecture in which search, identity, document handling, or analytics use proven building blocks instead of all-custom implementations.[^52]

A fifth efficiency is faster retirement of duplicate legacy spend once safe. The FY 2026 EPA appendix says about $55.1 million in current annual NextGen CM/ECF requirements would eventually be replaced by the new system's O&M and related requirements. That implies there is a real savings opportunity, but it only materializes if rollout is fast enough and standardized enough to stop paying both old-system and new-system costs for long periods. The risk, of course, is that attempting to optimize too aggressively for quick savings could endanger continuity in the courts. So this is a real efficiency lever, but only under careful staged migration.[^13]

Taken together, the sources suggest a credible lower-cost path for the judiciary would look like this: reuse DAWSON or other open-source components where they fit; buy commodity capabilities where they are mature; standardize workflows and schemas nationwide; enforce smaller product teams and tighter scope; keep cloud/shared services central; and focus policy energy on reducing local customization and shortening the dual-run period. That would not make the program "cheap," because the national judiciary is fundamentally large and heterogeneous. But it could plausibly reduce avoidable cost growth and improve the odds that the $700-$800 million estimate does not rise further.[^53]

## Bottom line and open questions

The most rigorous answer is that DAWSON and PACER modernization are not the same project at different prices. DAWSON was a focused one-court rebuild with a modern cloud/open-source/API posture, a controlled procurement vehicle, small teams, and limited migration scope. PACER modernization is a national judicial platform-replacement program that must simultaneously serve more than 200 courts, 5.3 million accounts, 621 million annual requests, and a corpus of more than 1 billion documents, while unwinding legacy Perl and Informix components, reducing customization sprawl, protecting sealed records, and keeping the courts running during migration. On those facts alone, a very large cost difference is expected.[^54]

At the same time, the official record does support two important criticisms of the PACER number. First, public comparisons often overstate the gap by comparing DAWSON's initial build to PACER's modernization plus ongoing operations, which is not an apples-to-apples cost basis. Second, the judiciary's own consultants identified concrete opportunities for cheaper delivery through reuse, standardization, and smaller agile teams. So the right conclusion is neither "the PACER estimate proves waste" nor "the comparison is meaningless." The right conclusion is that the projects differ enough to justify a very large gap, but the judiciary likely still has meaningful room to reduce cost through architecture and governance choices already identified in its own source documents.[^55]

Open questions remain. The public documents do not provide a fully itemized labor and contractor mix for either the PACER modernization estimate or the DAWSON build, so one cannot precisely separate software engineering from program-management, security, cloud, and transition costs. The exact portion of the Tax Court's post-2025 five-year IT contract that is specifically DAWSON application sustainment, as opposed to broader IT support, is also not entirely clear from the budget object classifications alone. And the judiciary itself says modernization estimates will be refined as the agile project progresses, which means the $700-$800 million figure is not the last word. Those uncertainties counsel against excessive precision, but they do not change the central finding: the gap is mostly driven by scope, migration, security, and coexistence costs, with some remaining opportunity for the judiciary to deliver the national program more efficiently.[^56]
